🧭 Market Structure

Understanding Market Structure

What is Market Structure?
Market structure is the framework that describes how price moves through time, creating patterns of highs and lows. It's the foundation of technical analysis and helps traders understand whether the market is trending or consolidating.

Market structure reveals the battle between buyers and sellers. By understanding how price creates structure, you can identify the dominant force in the market and position yourself accordingly. Every price movement leaves behind clues about future direction.

How Price Moves: Trending vs Ranging Markets

Trending Markets: Price creates a series of higher highs and higher lows (uptrend) or lower lows and lower highs (downtrend). These markets offer the best trading opportunities as they provide clear direction and momentum.

Ranging Markets: Price oscillates between established support and resistance levels without creating new structural points. These markets require different strategies, typically focused on mean reversion rather than momentum.

Key Insight: Approximately 70% of the time, markets are ranging. The skilled trader waits patiently for trending conditions and strikes when structure confirms direction.

Identifying Highs, Lows, and Swing Points

Swing points are the pivotal moments where price reverses direction, creating peaks (highs) and troughs (lows). These points define market structure and provide reference points for future analysis.

  • Swing High: A candlestick high that is higher than the highs of the candles immediately before and after it
  • Swing Low: A candlestick low that is lower than the lows of the candles immediately before and after it
  • Higher High (HH): A swing high that exceeds the previous swing high
  • Higher Low (HL): A swing low that is higher than the previous swing low
  • Lower Low (LL): A swing low that breaks below the previous swing low
  • Lower High (LH): A swing high that fails to exceed the previous swing high

BOS and CHOCH

These two concepts are critical for understanding market momentum and potential reversals. They tell you whether the current trend is likely to continue or if a reversal is imminent.

BOS (Break of Structure)

A Break of Structure occurs when price breaks beyond a previous swing high (in an uptrend) or swing low (in a downtrend). This confirms that the trend is continuing and provides confidence for trend-following entries.

Bullish BOS: Price breaks above the previous swing high, confirming upward momentum.

Bearish BOS: Price breaks below the previous swing low, confirming downward momentum.

Trading Tip: After a BOS, wait for price to retrace into a demand zone (uptrend) or supply zone (downtrend) before entering. This gives you better risk-to-reward ratios.

CHOCH (Change of Character)

A Change of Character signals a potential trend reversal. It occurs when price breaks the opposite structure—for example, in an uptrend, price breaks below a previous swing low instead of making a higher low.

Bullish CHOCH: During a downtrend, price breaks above a previous swing high.

Bearish CHOCH: During an uptrend, price breaks below a previous swing low.

Important: CHOCH alone is not enough confirmation for a reversal. Wait for price to create a new structure in the opposite direction (forming a new high/low) before committing to reversal trades.

Internal and External Structure

Understanding the relationship between different timeframe structures is crucial for accurate market analysis. This multi-timeframe approach helps you avoid false signals and align your trades with the dominant trend.

External Structure (Higher Timeframe)

External structure represents the major swing points visible on higher timeframes (4H, Daily, Weekly). These are the primary levels that institutional traders and smart money are watching. External structure defines the overall trend and major support/resistance zones.

Think of external structure as the "big picture" that governs overall market direction. It's slower to change and provides the context for all lower timeframe movements.

Internal Structure (Lower Timeframe)

Internal structure consists of the smaller swing points that form within the legs of external structure. These are visible on lower timeframes (15m, 1H) and provide precise entry opportunities within the context of the higher timeframe trend.

Internal structure helps you refine your entries and exits. While external structure tells you the direction, internal structure tells you the timing.

The Golden Rule: Trade internal structure in the direction of external structure. When higher timeframe structure is bullish, look for bullish internal structure breaks on lower timeframes for entries. This alignment dramatically improves your win rate.

⚖️ Supply and Demand

Understanding Supply & Demand Zones

What are Supply and Demand Zones?
Supply and demand zones are areas on the chart where significant buying or selling pressure caused an imbalance, resulting in strong price movements away from those levels. These zones represent areas where institutional orders were placed.

Unlike traditional support and resistance lines, supply and demand zones are areas rather than precise levels. They represent regions where large players accumulated or distributed positions, leaving behind footprints of their activity.

Demand Zone (Buying Area): An area where buyers overwhelmed sellers, causing price to move aggressively higher. These are potential support zones where price may bounce again.

Supply Zone (Selling Area): An area where sellers overwhelmed buyers, causing price to move aggressively lower. These are potential resistance zones where price may reverse again.

How Price Reacts to These Zones

When price returns to a supply or demand zone, several scenarios can occur:

  • Respect: Price bounces off the zone, confirming it's still valid
  • Mitigation: Price moves into the zone and partially fills orders before reversing
  • Violation: Price moves completely through the zone, invalidating it
Pro Tip: Fresh zones (untested after creation) have the highest probability of causing reactions. Once a zone has been tested multiple times, its strength diminishes.

Order Flow

Order flow is the real-time analysis of buying and selling pressure in the market. It reveals which side (buyers or sellers) is in control and helps predict future price movement.

Reading Price Movement

Bullish Order Flow:

  • Strong green candles with small wicks
  • Consecutive higher highs and higher lows
  • Quick moves through resistance
  • Shallow pullbacks
  • Volume increasing on upward moves

Bearish Order Flow:

  • Strong red candles with small wicks
  • Consecutive lower lows and lower highs
  • Quick moves through support
  • Shallow pullbacks
  • Volume increasing on downward moves

Identifying Momentum and Exhaustion

Momentum Signals:

  • Large candle bodies relative to wicks
  • Consecutive candles in the same direction
  • Breaking through zones with ease
  • Increasing range of candles

Exhaustion Signals:

  • Long wicks with small bodies (rejection)
  • Slowing momentum (smaller candle ranges)
  • Multiple attempts to break a level with failure
  • Divergence between price and volume
Warning: Exhaustion doesn't guarantee an immediate reversal. Price can remain in exhaustion for extended periods before finally reversing. Wait for structure confirmation.

Order Blocks

What is an Order Block?
An order block is the last up-close candle before a bearish move (bearish OB) or the last down-close candle before a bullish move (bullish OB). It represents where institutional orders were placed before causing a significant move in the opposite direction.

Order blocks are more refined than simple supply and demand zones. They pinpoint the exact candle or cluster of candles where smart money initiated their positions, making them highly accurate for entries.

How to Identify Valid Order Blocks

Not all order blocks are created equal. Valid order blocks have specific characteristics:

  • Strong Impulse: The move away from the OB must be strong and decisive
  • Clean Structure: Minimal consolidation before the impulse move
  • Volume: Increased volume on the impulse candle away from the OB
  • Freshness: Untested OBs have higher probability
  • Alignment: OB aligns with higher timeframe structure

How to Choose Order Blocks

When multiple order blocks are present, use these criteria to select the best one:

1. Alignment with Structure: The OB should align with a BOS or CHOCH. An OB that forms after a structural break has much higher probability.

2. Liquidity Before OB: Look for liquidity sweeps (equal highs/lows taken) just before the OB forms. Smart money often grabs liquidity before placing their orders.

3. Confirmation with BOS/CHOCH: The OB should be followed by a clear break of structure in the expected direction, confirming that orders are being filled.

4. Proper Mitigation and Imbalance: The OB should show imbalance (FVG) within or near it, and price should partially mitigate it rather than blowing straight through.

Golden Rule for Order Blocks: The best order blocks combine all four criteria: structural alignment, liquidity grab, BOS confirmation, and imbalance. Never settle for OBs that only meet one or two criteria.

💧 Liquidity and Inducement

Types of Liquidity

What is Liquidity?
Liquidity refers to clusters of stop-loss orders and pending orders that accumulate at obvious price levels. Smart money (institutions) targets these liquidity pools to fill their large orders efficiently.

Understanding liquidity is like seeing the matrix of the market. While retail traders place stops at obvious levels, smart money hunts these stops before moving price in their intended direction. This creates the "stop hunts" and "false breakouts" that trap uninformed traders.

Equal Highs/Lows

Equal highs or equal lows are swing points that form at approximately the same price level. These areas are liquidity magnets because retail traders place their stop-losses just above equal highs (for shorts) or below equal lows (for longs).

Trading Tip: When you see equal highs in an uptrend, expect price to sweep above them before reversing. When you see equal lows in a downtrend, expect price to sweep below them before reversing. This is classic liquidity engineering.

Trendline Liquidity

Trendlines are another obvious place where retail traders congregate. Smart money knows that thousands of traders are watching the same trendlines and placing their orders there. Breaking below an upward trendline or above a downward trendline triggers mass stop-losses and provides liquidity.

Session Liquidity

Session highs and lows (from Asian, London, or New York sessions) attract liquidity. The previous day's high/low, previous week's high/low, and previous month's high/low are all significant liquidity levels that price often targets.

Internal vs External Liquidity

Internal Liquidity: Liquidity that sits within the current range. This includes equal highs/lows and swing points inside the larger structure. Internal liquidity is often used for inducement—grabbing stops to fuel the move toward external liquidity.

External Liquidity: Liquidity that sits beyond the current structure highs or lows. This is the primary target for smart money. Major structural highs/lows hold the most external liquidity and are the ultimate destinations for price moves.

Liquidity Concept: Price moves from liquidity to liquidity. It sweeps internal liquidity (inducement) to gather momentum, then targets external liquidity (the real objective). Understanding this flow gives you a massive edge.

How to Spot Liquidity

Identifying liquidity zones is a critical skill that separates profitable traders from the rest. Here's how to systematically find liquidity on any chart:

  • Mark All Swing Highs and Lows: These are natural stop-loss placement areas
  • Identify Equal Levels: Multiple touches at the same price create liquidity pools
  • Note Psychological Levels: Round numbers (e.g., $50,000 for Bitcoin) attract orders
  • Previous Session Extremes: Daily, weekly, monthly highs and lows
  • Trendline Breaks: Where retail traders are stopped out
  • Gaps: Overnight or weekend gaps often see liquidity sweeps

Areas Where Traders' Stop-Losses Are Likely Placed

Put yourself in the shoes of retail traders. Where would they place stops?

  • Just above/below obvious swing points
  • Beyond trendlines they're trading
  • Above/below consolidation ranges (breakout traders' stops)
  • At round psychological numbers
  • Previous day's high/low (common for day traders)

Points Where Smart Money Targets Liquidity Pools

Smart money doesn't randomly hunt liquidity. They target specific pools that provide the most efficiency for their large orders:

  • Breakout Trap Zones: Areas where false breakouts previously occurred
  • Multiple Timeframe Confluence: Where liquidity aligns across several timeframes
  • Pre-News Levels: Stops accumulated before major news events
  • Old Swing Points: Significant highs/lows from weeks or months ago
  • Retail Patterns: Head and shoulders, triangles, wedges—patterns that retail trades
Critical Insight: Smart money doesn't fight retail—they use retail as exit liquidity. When everyone is buying, institutions are selling to them. When everyone is selling in panic, institutions are buying from them.

Internal and External Liquidity Deep Dive

The relationship between internal and external liquidity creates the roadmap for price movement. Mastering this concept transforms how you read charts.

Internal Liquidity (Inducement)

Internal liquidity serves as bait. Smart money sweeps these levels to:

  • Trigger retail stops and create momentum
  • Generate the fuel needed to reach external liquidity
  • Create the appearance of a reversal (false signal)
  • Establish optimal entry positions

Example: In a bullish market structure, price might sweep below an internal low (taking out stops), creating a false breakdown. This inducement generates selling pressure that smart money absorbs, then price reverses sharply upward toward external liquidity above.

External Liquidity (True Target)

External liquidity represents the real objective. These are the major stops and orders that sit beyond current structure:

  • All-time highs or lows
  • Major swing points on daily/weekly charts
  • Month/year opening levels
  • Significant psychological levels

Example: Bitcoin trading at $42,000 with previous high at $44,000. The $44,000 level is external liquidity. Price will likely sweep internal lows first (perhaps $41,500), then drive toward $44,000+ to grab external liquidity.

⚡ Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

What is FVG?

Fair Value Gap Definition
A Fair Value Gap is an imbalance in price action where price moves so quickly that it leaves behind an unfilled area on the chart. This gap represents inefficiency—an area where fair value was not established because one side (buyers or sellers) completely overwhelmed the other.

FVGs appear as three-candle patterns where the wicks of candles 1 and 3 don't overlap, leaving a visible gap. This gap represents "fair value" that price tends to return to and fill before continuing in the original direction.

Price Imbalance and Rebalancing Concept

Think of FVGs as debts that the market must repay. When price creates an imbalance by moving too quickly, it eventually returns to "balance the books" by filling that gap.

Rebalancing occurs when:

  • Price returns to the FVG zone
  • At least 50% of the gap is filled
  • Often, the entire gap gets filled before price continues
Pro Tip: Not all FVGs get filled immediately. Some remain open for days or weeks. The larger the timeframe of the FVG, the more significant it is and the higher the probability it will eventually be filled.

Buy-Sell Imbalance

Buy-Side Imbalance (Bullish FVG)

A buy-side imbalance occurs when buyers are so aggressive that they push price up rapidly, leaving a gap. This creates a bullish FVG—an area of unfilled value below current price.

Characteristics:

  • Three consecutive candles with upward momentum
  • Gap between candle 1's high and candle 3's low
  • Strong bullish candle in the middle (candle 2)
  • Represents demand exceeding supply dramatically

Trading Application: When price retraces into a bullish FVG after a BOS, it presents a buying opportunity. The FVG acts as support, and you can enter long positions within the gap with stops below it.

Sell-Side Imbalance (Bearish FVG)

A sell-side imbalance occurs when sellers are so aggressive that they push price down rapidly, leaving a gap. This creates a bearish FVG—an area of unfilled value above current price.

Characteristics:

  • Three consecutive candles with downward momentum
  • Gap between candle 1's low and candle 3's high
  • Strong bearish candle in the middle (candle 2)
  • Represents supply exceeding demand dramatically

Trading Application: When price retraces into a bearish FVG after a BOS, it presents a selling opportunity. The FVG acts as resistance, and you can enter short positions within the gap with stops above it.

Premium and Discount

Every market range can be divided into premium and discount zones. This concept helps you buy low (discount) and sell high (premium), increasing your win rate and risk-reward ratios.

Understanding Equilibrium

Draw the range from a significant low to a significant high. The 50% level is equilibrium—fair value. Everything above 50% is premium (expensive). Everything below 50% is discount (cheap).

The Range Breakdown:

  • 100% - 75%: Extreme Premium
  • 75% - 50%: Premium
  • 50%: Equilibrium (Fair Value)
  • 50% - 25%: Discount
  • 25% - 0%: Extreme Discount

Premium Zone (Sell Area Above Equilibrium)

The premium zone is where you look for short entries if you're bearish. This is where price is "expensive" relative to the range, making it an ideal area to sell.

Important: Never short just because price is in premium. You need confirmation that sellers are taking control (CHOCH, rejection candle, etc.). Premium alone is not an entry signal.

Discount Zone (Buy Area Below Equilibrium)

The discount zone is where you look for long entries if you're bullish. This is where price is "cheap" relative to the range, making it an ideal area to buy.

Golden Rule: In an uptrend, only buy in discount. In a downtrend, only sell in premium. This simple rule prevents you from chasing price and improves your entries dramatically.

🎯 Entry Models

Continuation Entry

The continuation entry is one of the highest probability setups because you're trading with the established trend after it has proven itself with a clear Break of Structure.

Continuation Entry Definition
A continuation entry occurs after price breaks structure in the direction of the trend (BOS), then retraces into a valid order block or FVG, providing an opportunity to enter in the direction of the trend continuation.

Step-by-Step Continuation Entry Process

  1. Identify the Trend: Confirm you're in an uptrend (HH, HL) or downtrend (LL, LH) on your chosen timeframe
  2. Wait for BOS: Price must break the previous swing high (uptrend) or swing low (downtrend) to confirm continuation
  3. Mark the Order Block/FVG: Identify the last opposing candle before the BOS move and any FVGs created during the impulse
  4. Wait for Retracement: Be patient as price pulls back into your marked zone. Don't chase the initial impulse
  5. Look for Confirmation: When price reaches the OB/FVG, wait for rejection signs or a lower timeframe BOS
  6. Enter the Trade: Place entry within the OB/FVG with stop loss below the OB (longs) or above the OB (shorts)
  7. Set Targets: Target the next liquidity level, structural high/low, or use a risk-reward ratio of at least 1:3

CHOCH Entry

The CHOCH entry is used when you anticipate a trend reversal. This is a more advanced and riskier entry model, but when executed correctly, it can catch the beginning of major trend reversals.

CHOCH Entry Definition
A CHOCH entry occurs after price breaks structure in the opposite direction of the previous trend, signaling a Change of Character. This suggests the trend may be reversing, creating an opportunity to enter early in the new direction.

Step-by-Step CHOCH Entry Process

  1. Identify Weakness: Look for signs the current trend is weakening
  2. Spot the CHOCH: Price breaks opposite structure
  3. Wait for Structure Formation: Wait for price to create a new high or low in the new direction
  4. Identify Order Block: Mark the order block that formed before the CHOCH move
  5. Wait for Retracement: Price should retrace back to the OB/FVG
  6. Confirm with Lower Timeframe BOS: Drop to a lower timeframe and wait for a BOS
  7. Enter with Caution: Enter within the OB with stops beyond the previous structure point
Critical Warning: CHOCH entries are counter-trend initially, making them riskier. Never rely on CHOCH alone. You must see structure confirmation before fully committing.

Flip Entry

The flip entry is one of the most powerful setups because it represents zones where the market has completely changed character.

Flip Entry Definition
A flip entry occurs when a previous supply zone (resistance) is broken and becomes a demand zone (support), or when a previous demand zone (support) is broken and becomes a supply zone (resistance).

Entry on Retest of Flipped Structure

  1. Identify the Original Zone: Mark significant supply or demand zones
  2. Wait for the Break: Price must decisively break through the zone
  3. Confirm the Flip: Price should move away and create new structure
  4. Wait for Retest: Be patient as price returns to test the flipped zone
  5. Look for Rejection: Watch for strong rejection candles
  6. Enter the Trade: Enter within the flipped zone with stops beyond the zone boundary

Chapter 7

💰 Risk Management

The Foundation of Long-Term Success

You can have the best technical analysis in the world, but without proper risk management, you will eventually lose everything.

Fundamental Truth: Your job as a trader is not to make money on every trade. Your job is to execute your edge consistently while protecting your capital.

Risk-to-Reward Ratio

Understanding RR Ratios

1:1 RR: Risk $100 to make $100 (break-even requires 50% win rate)

1:2 RR: Risk $100 to make $200 (break-even requires 33% win rate)

1:3 RR: Risk $100 to make $300 (break-even requires 25% win rate)

1:5 RR: Risk $100 to make $500 (break-even requires 17% win rate)

The Math That Changes Everything: With a 1:3 RR, you can be wrong 60% of the time and still be profitable.

Fixed Percentage Per Trade

The 1-2% Rule

Why 1-2% is Optimal:

  • Survivability: You can withstand 50 consecutive losses
  • Psychological Health: Individual losses don't create emotional damage
  • Compound Growth: Small consistent gains compound dramatically
  • Professional Standard: This is what institutional traders use

Position Sizing Formula

Position Size = (Account Size × Risk %) / (Entry Price - Stop Loss)

Example:

  • Account Size: $10,000
  • Risk Per Trade: 2% ($200)
  • Entry Price: $50,000
  • Stop Loss: $49,500
  • Risk Per Unit: $500
  • Position Size: $200 ÷ $500 = 0.4 units (BTC)

Stop Loss Placement

Strategic Stop Loss Placement

For Long Positions:

  • Below the order block you entered from
  • Below the liquidity sweep low
  • Below the FVG zone
  • Give it breathing room

For Short Positions:

  • Above the order block
  • Above the liquidity sweep high
  • Above the FVG zone
  • Give it breathing room
Never Move Your Stop Loss Further Away: This is how traders blow accounts.
Trailing Stop Strategy: As price continues in your favor, trail your stop below each new swing low (longs) or above each new swing high (shorts). This locks in profits while giving the trend room to breathe.

Emotional Discipline

Technical skills get you into trades. Emotional discipline keeps you profitable long-term. The psychology of trading is often more important than the technical analysis.

The Psychological Challenges of Trading

Fear: Fear of losing money can prevent you from taking valid setups. Combat this by risking only 1-2% per trade.

Greed: Greed causes you to overtrade or risk too much. Combat this by having strict rules.

Revenge Trading: After a loss, the urge to "get it back" leads to impulsive trades. Combat this by stepping away after a loss.

FOMO: Seeing price move without you creates urgency to enter late. Combat this by accepting that you'll miss trades.

Overconfidence: After a winning streak, traders often increase risk. Combat this by maintaining the same process.

Final Risk Management Principles

The Non-Negotiable Rules

  1. Never risk more than 2% per trade — No exceptions
  2. Never risk more than 6% total — Maximum of 3 concurrent trades
  3. Minimum 1:3 RR on every trade — If you can't get 1:3, don't take the trade
  4. Always use stop losses — Never hope a losing trade will come back
  5. Never move stops further away — This is how accounts die
  6. Take breaks after losses — Don't revenge trade
  7. Journal every trade — Without data, you can't improve
  8. Follow your rules religiously — Consistency beats brilliance
  9. Accept that losses happen — Even the best traders have 40-50% losses
