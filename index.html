Mu'tazilism: Stressed divine justice and human free will, giving significant role to reason in understanding faith.

وَمَا خَلَقْتُ الْجِنَّ وَالْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ

And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me.

— Quran 51:56

Islamic Mysticism (Sufism)

Sufism represents the mystical dimension of Islam, emphasizing the inner spiritual journey toward Allah. Sufis seek to purify the heart and achieve direct spiritual experience of the divine.

Stages of Sufi Spiritual Development:

  • Tawbah (Repentance) - Turning away from sin
  • Wara (Scrupulousness) - Avoiding doubtful matters
  • Zuhd (Asceticism) - Detachment from worldly desires
  • Sabr (Patience) - Perseverance in trials
  • Tawakkul (Trust) - Complete reliance on Allah
  • Rida (Contentment) - Satisfaction with Allah's decree

The Question of Divine Attributes

Islamic theologians extensively debated the nature of Allah's attributes, such as knowledge, power, and mercy. The central question was whether these attributes are identical to Allah's essence or distinct from it.

"Allah has ninety-nine names, one hundred minus one, and whoever encompasses them will enter Paradise."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

The Islamic Calendar and Festivals

The Islamic calendar, also known as the Hijri calendar, is a lunar calendar consisting of 12 months and approximately 354-355 days. It begins with the Hijra (migration) of Prophet Muhammad from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE.

The Islamic Months

The Twelve Islamic Months:

  • Muharram - The sacred month, beginning of the Islamic year
  • Safar - The empty or void month
  • Rabi' al-Awwal - The first spring month
  • Rabi' al-Thani - The second spring month
  • Jumada al-Awwal - The first dry month
  • Jumada al-Thani - The second dry month
  • Rajab - The respected month
  • Sha'ban - The month of separation
  • Ramadan - The month of fasting
  • Shawwal - The month of hunting
  • Dhul Qi'dah - The month of rest
  • Dhul Hijjah - The month of pilgrimage

Major Islamic Festivals

Eid al-Fitr (Festival of Breaking the Fast)

Celebrated on the first day of Shawwal, marking the end of Ramadan. Muslims gather for special prayers, give charity (Zakat al-Fitr), exchange gifts, and enjoy festive meals with family and friends.

Eid al-Adha (Festival of Sacrifice)

Observed on the 10th day of Dhul Hijjah, commemorating Prophet Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son in obedience to Allah. Muslims who can afford it sacrifice an animal and distribute the meat to family, friends, and the poor.

فَصَلِّ لِرَبِّكَ وَانْحَرْ

So pray to your Lord and sacrifice [to Him alone].

— Quran 108:2

Sacred Months

Four months in the Islamic calendar are considered sacred: Muharram, Rajab, Dhul Qi'dah, and Dhul Hijjah. During these months, fighting was traditionally prohibited, and spiritual activities were emphasized.

Other Important Observances

Significant Islamic Dates:

  • Ashura (10th Muharram) - Day of fasting and remembrance
  • Mawlid al-Nabi - Birthday of Prophet Muhammad
  • Laylat al-Qadr - The Night of Power during Ramadan
  • Laylat al-Mi'raj - The Night Journey of the Prophet
  • Day of Arafat (9th Dhul Hijjah) - Day of Hajj pilgrimage

The Significance of Lunar Calendar

The use of a lunar calendar means that Islamic festivals rotate through different seasons over the years, ensuring that no particular season is permanently associated with religious observances.
