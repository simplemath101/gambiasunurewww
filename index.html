Chapter 1

How I Start My Trading Day

Consistency in trading begins with consistency in preparation. Every successful trade starts before the market even opens.

The Real Trading Window

Not all hours are created equal in trading. The "real trading window" refers to periods when market liquidity is at its peak and institutional activity is highest. This is when the best setups form and execute.

Why does this matter? Because during low-liquidity periods, price can move erratically without clear structure. Spreads widen, slippage increases, and patterns become unreliable. Smart traders focus their energy when smart money is most active.

Identifying Optimal Liquidity Times

The forex and gold markets operate 24 hours, but three main sessions drive the majority of volume:

Session Time (GMT) Key Characteristics
London Session 8:00 - 17:00 Highest volume, clear trends, major moves
New York Session 13:00 - 22:00 Strong volatility, US news impact
London/NY Overlap 13:00 - 17:00 Peak liquidity, best trade execution

The Golden Window

The London/New York overlap (13:00-17:00 GMT) is the most important trading period. This 4-hour window sees the highest institutional participation, tightest spreads, and most reliable price action. If you can only trade for a few hours per day, make it these hours.

My Pre-Market Preparation Checklist

Before placing a single trade, I go through this systematic checklist every day:

Daily Pre-Market Routine

  1. Check Economic Calendar – Identify high-impact news events (NFP, FOMC, CPI, GDP). Avoid trading 30 minutes before and after major announcements.
  2. Review Higher Time Frame Bias – Open 4H and 1H charts for your pairs. Determine the current trend and market structure. Are we bullish, bearish, or ranging?
  3. Mark Key Levels – Identify and draw:
    • Recent highs and lows (liquidity pools)
    • Order blocks (last bullish/bearish candle before strong moves)
    • Fair value gaps (imbalances)
    • Support and resistance zones
  4. Set Daily Bias – Based on HTF analysis, am I looking for longs, shorts, or staying flat today?
  5. Wait for London Open or NY Open – Don't force trades during Asian session unless specifically trading that market.

Why This Matters

Traders who skip preparation tend to:

  • Trade impulsively without context
  • Get caught in choppy, low-volume periods
  • Miss the forest for the trees (no HTF bias)
  • Enter positions right before major news events

By starting your day with structure and clarity, you dramatically increase your edge. You know what you're looking for, when to look for it, and when to step aside.

Pro Tip

Create a written or digital trading journal. Each morning, write down your bias, key levels, and trade plan. This builds discipline and provides valuable data for reviewing your performance.

Chapter 2

Pairs I Trade

Not all currency pairs are created equal. The best trades come from the most liquid, structurally clear instruments.

My Core Pairs

I focus exclusively on three instruments that consistently provide the best setups:

Primary Trading Instruments

  • EUR/USD – The Euro vs. US Dollar
  • GBP/USD – The British Pound vs. US Dollar
  • XAU/USD – Gold vs. US Dollar

Why These Pairs?

1. EUR/USD – The King of Forex

EUR/USD is the most traded currency pair in the world, accounting for roughly 25% of all forex transactions. This means:

  • Maximum liquidity – Orders fill instantly with minimal slippage
  • Tight spreads – Often just 0.5-1.0 pips with good brokers
  • Clean structure – Institutional flow creates clear patterns
  • Predictable behavior – Responds well to SMC concepts

2. GBP/USD – Volatility With Structure

Known as "Cable," GBP/USD offers more volatility than EUR/USD while maintaining institutional liquidity:

  • Larger moves – Great for hitting 1:7+ risk-to-reward targets
  • Respects key levels – Order blocks and liquidity zones are highly reliable
  • Active during London session – Aligns perfectly with our trading window
  • Trending nature – When it moves, it moves with conviction

3. XAU/USD – Gold

Gold is my favorite instrument for SMC trading. It exhibits the clearest institutional footprints:

  • Massive liquidity pools – Stop hunts are obvious and tradeable
  • Respects order blocks religiously – Institutional zones act like magnets
  • High volatility – Easily achieves 1:10+ R:R with proper entries
  • Safe-haven flows – Reacts strongly to global economic events

Why I Avoid Other Pairs

Exotic pairs (e.g., USD/TRY, EUR/ZAR) and minor crosses (e.g., EUR/GBP, AUD/NZD) suffer from:

  • Wide spreads that eat into profits
  • Low liquidity causing erratic price action
  • Unclear market structure
  • Higher susceptibility to manipulation

Stick to the majors and gold. Master three instruments rather than spreading yourself thin across dozens.

Broker Considerations

The quality of your broker directly impacts your results. Look for:

  • Tight spreads – EUR/USD should be under 1 pip, GBP/USD under 1.5 pips, Gold under $0.20
  • Fast execution – Orders should fill in milliseconds
  • Regulation – Choose brokers regulated by FCA, ASIC, or equivalent authorities
  • No stop hunting – Reputable brokers don't manipulate your stops

Spread Impact Example

If you risk $100 to make $700 (1:7 R:R), but your broker has a 3-pip spread on EUR/USD versus a 0.8-pip spread elsewhere, you're losing $22 in additional costs per trade. Over 100 trades, that's $2,200 in pure waste.

Can I Trade Crypto with This Strategy?

Yes, SMC concepts apply to cryptocurrencies, especially major pairs like BTC/USD and ETH/USD. However, be aware:

  • Crypto markets have higher volatility and wider swings
  • Liquidity can be lower, especially on smaller exchanges
  • 24/7 markets mean no clear "session" structure
  • Leverage regulations vary significantly by region

If you choose to trade crypto, apply the same principles: focus on the most liquid instruments and high-quality setups only.

Chapter 3

Time Frames

Using multiple time frames correctly is what separates amateur traders from professionals.

The Three-Timeframe Approach

Successful trading requires understanding what's happening at different time horizons. I use a simple yet powerful three-timeframe structure:

Time Frame Purpose What I Look For
4-Hour (H4) Macro bias Overall trend, major structure breaks, key swing points
1-Hour (H1) Directional bias Current market structure, order blocks, liquidity zones
15-Minute (M15) Entry trigger Precise entry setups, liquidity sweeps, confirmations

The 4-Hour Chart (H4) – Your Compass

The 4-hour chart shows you the bigger picture. Before you consider any trade, ask yourself:

  • Is the H4 chart trending or ranging?
  • Where is the most recent market structure break?
  • What are the major swing highs and lows?
  • Are we near significant support or resistance?

Think of H4 as your compass. It tells you the general direction institutional money is moving. Never trade against the H4 bias unless you have extremely strong confluence.

Example: H4 Analysis

EUR/USD on H4 shows a clear uptrend with higher highs and higher lows. The most recent swing low is at 1.0800, and price recently broke above 1.1000 resistance. Current price: 1.1050.

Bias: Bullish. I'm only looking for long (buy) setups on lower time frames.

The 1-Hour Chart (H1) – Your Tactical Map

Once you have your H4 bias, the 1-hour chart provides tactical context. Here you identify:

  • The current market structure (bullish, bearish, or consolidating)
  • Order blocks – zones where institutions placed large orders
  • Liquidity pools – clusters of stops above highs or below lows
  • Fair value gaps – imbalances that price may fill

The H1 chart answers: "Within the larger H4 trend, where exactly should I look for entries?"

H1 Checklist

  • Does H1 structure align with H4 bias?
  • Where is the last bullish/bearish order block?
  • Are there obvious liquidity pools to target or avoid?
  • Has there been a recent market structure shift?

The 15-Minute Chart (M15) – Your Trigger

This is where trades are executed. The M15 chart is for precision entries. You never trade on M15 without first having:

  1. Clear H4 bias
  2. Supportive H1 structure
  3. A specific order block or zone identified

On M15, you're looking for the liquidity sweep + market structure shift (MSS) + confirmation pattern that signals institutional entry.

Example: Multi-Timeframe Trade Setup

H4 Bias: GBP/USD is bullish. Price broke above 1.2800 and is making higher highs.

H1 Context: Price pulled back to a bullish order block at 1.2750. This zone previously acted as resistance and should now be support.

M15 Entry: Price sweeps below 1.2750 (liquidity grab), then closes back above it with a strong bullish candle. This is your entry trigger.

Entry: 1.2755 | Stop Loss: 1.2720 (35 pips) | Take Profit: 1.3000 (245 pips) | R:R: 1:7

Why This Matters

Traders who use only one time frame suffer from:

  • No context – They don't know if they're trading with or against the trend
  • Premature entries – They enter before the setup fully forms
  • Poor stop placement – They don't know where structure truly invalidates

By combining H4, H1, and M15, you gain:

  • High conviction in your direction
  • Precise entry points
  • Logical stop loss placement
  • Clear profit targets based on structure

The Golden Rule

H4 tells you WHAT (direction), H1 tells you WHERE (zones), M15 tells you WHEN (timing).

Chapter 4

Entries & Exits

Perfect entries and disciplined exits are what turn analysis into profit.

The SMC Entry Formula

Every trade must meet three criteria before execution:

Entry Requirements (All Three Must Be Present)

  1. Liquidity Sweep – Price takes out obvious highs or lows where retail stops are clustered
  2. Market Structure Shift (MSS) – A clear break of the recent internal structure in the opposite direction
  3. Confirmation – A strong momentum candle (or candles) showing institutional interest

1. Liquidity Sweep Explained

Institutions need liquidity to fill large orders. They often push price to areas where retail traders have placed stop losses, triggering those stops to provide the liquidity needed. This creates a "sweep" or "stop hunt."

How to identify:

  • Look for recent swing highs (in downtrends) or swing lows (in uptrends)
  • Watch for price to push just beyond these levels with wicks
  • The sweep often happens quickly with immediate reversal
  • Volume typically spikes during the sweep

2. Market Structure Shift (MSS)

An MSS occurs when price breaks the most recent higher low (in an uptrend) or lower high (in a downtrend). This signals a potential change in short-term direction and institutional repositioning.

For bullish setups: After sweeping lows, price must break above the most recent lower high to confirm bullish MSS.

For bearish setups: After sweeping highs, price must break below the most recent higher low to confirm bearish MSS.

3. Confirmation

Never enter on the sweep alone. Wait for confirmation through:

  • Strong momentum candle(s) in your intended direction
  • Candle closes beyond the MSS level
  • Rejection wicks showing institutional defense of the level

Entry Checklist

  • ✓ H4 bias aligns with my trade direction
  • ✓ H1 shows clear order block or liquidity zone
  • ✓ M15 shows liquidity sweep
  • ✓ M15 shows market structure shift
  • ✓ Strong confirmation candle(s) present
  • ✓ Risk-to-reward is minimum 1:7
  • ✓ No major news in next 2 hours

If all boxes aren't checked, DO NOT TRADE.

Stop Loss Placement

Your stop loss must be placed where your trade idea is proven wrong structurally. Common placements:

  • Beyond the sweep wick – Place stop 5-10 pips past the liquidity sweep low (for longs) or high (for shorts)
  • Beyond the order block – If entering from an order block, stop goes below the block with buffer
  • Beyond structure break – Place stop beyond the level that would invalidate your MSS

Never Use Arbitrary Stops

Don't place stops based on "I want to risk $50" or "20 pips feels right." Stops must be based on market structure. If the structure-based stop is too large for your account, skip the trade—don't risk improper placement just to enter.

Take Profit Targets

Take profit should target the next significant liquidity pool or structural level:

  • Next opposite-side liquidity – Recent swing high (for longs) or swing low (for shorts)
  • Fair value gaps – Unfilled imbalances price tends to return to
  • Round numbers – Major psychological levels (1.3000, 1.2500, etc.)
  • Order block zones – Opposite-side institutional areas

Aim for minimum 1:7 risk-to-reward. If the nearest structural target doesn't offer 1:7, wait for a better setup.

Complete Trade Example: EUR/USD Long

Setup:

  • H4 Bias: Bullish structure, recent break above 1.1000
  • H1 Context: Bullish order block at 1.1050-1.1060 zone
  • M15 Trigger: Price sweeps to 1.1048 (below order block), then breaks back above with strong bullish candle to 1.1065

Execution:

  • Entry: 1.1065 (after confirmation candle close)
  • Stop Loss: 1.1035 (below sweep wick with buffer) = 30 pips risk
  • Take Profit: 1.1275 (next major liquidity high) = 210 pips reward
  • Risk-to-Reward: 1:7

Result: If risking $100 on this trade, potential profit is $700.

Exit Checklist

  • ✓ Stop loss placed beyond structural invalidation point
  • ✓ Take profit targets next liquidity pool or major level
  • ✓ Risk-to-reward calculated and meets 1:7 minimum
  • ✓ Partial profit strategy planned (optional: take 50% at 1:3, let rest run to 1:7+)
  • ✓ Trailing stop strategy if trade moves strongly in your favor

Partial Profits & Trade Management

Advanced traders often take partial profits along the way:

  • Scale out at 1:3 – Take 50% profit, move stop to breakeven on remainder
  • Trail remaining position – As price moves favorably, trail stop behind new structure
  • Final target at 1:7+ – Let the last portion run to major target

This approach locks in profits while maintaining exposure for larger wins.

Common Entry Mistakes to Avoid

  • Entering too early – Before all three criteria are met
  • FOMO entries – Jumping in because price is moving
  • Counter-trend trades – Trading against H4 bias without strong confluence
  • Ignoring confirmation – Entering on the sweep alone
  • Moving stops – Never move stop loss further away after entry

The Cardinal Sin

NEVER move your stop loss to give a losing trade "more room." If your stop is hit, your analysis was wrong. Accept the loss and move on. Moving stops is how accounts blow up.

Chapter 5

Risk & Reward

Risk management is not what you do after you're profitable—it's what makes you profitable.

The 1:7 Risk-to-Reward Target

The cornerstone of this strategy is achieving a minimum 1:7 risk-to-reward ratio on every trade. This means for every dollar you risk, you aim to make seven dollars.

Why 1:7?

  • You can be wrong 7 out of 10 times and still break even
  • A 50% win rate yields 250% profit on risk
  • Forces you to be selective and take only the best setups
  • Aligns with how institutional money trades (quality over quantity)

Win Rate Math

Scenario: 10 trades, 1:7 R:R, risking $100 per trade

40% Win Rate (4 wins, 6 losses):

  • Losses: 6 × $100 = -$600
  • Wins: 4 × $700 = +$2,800
  • Net Profit: +$2,200

With just 4 winners out of 10, you're up $2,200. This is the power of asymmetric risk-reward.

Risk Per Trade: 1-2% Maximum

Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account balance on a single trade. This is non-negotiable.

Why this matters:

  • Protects your account from catastrophic losses
  • Allows you to survive long losing streaks
  • Removes emotional pressure from individual trades
  • Professional standard across all successful traders

The Blowup Math

If you risk 10% per trade, just 7 consecutive losses wipes out 50% of your account. You would need 100% gains just to get back to breakeven. Don't let this be you.

Position Sizing Formula

To determine your position size, use this simple formula:

Position Size = (Account Balance × Risk %) ÷ Stop Loss Distance

Position Sizing Example

Account Balance: $10,000

Risk Per Trade: 1% = $100

Trade Setup: EUR/USD Long

Entry: 1.1065

Stop Loss: 1.1035

Stop Distance: 30 pips

Calculation:

For a standard lot (100,000 units), 1 pip = $10

30 pips = $300 risk per standard lot

To risk $100: $100 ÷ $300 = 0.33 standard lots = 33,000 units

Position Size: 0.33 lots (33,000 units)

Most brokers and trading platforms have position size calculators built in. Use them to avoid manual errors.

Account Growth Example

Let's see how 1% risk with 1:7 R:R compounds over time:

Starting Balance Wins (40% rate) Losses (60%) Ending Balance
$5,000 12 wins × $350 18 losses × $50 $8,300
$10,000 12 wins × $700 18 losses × $100 $16,600
$25,000 12 wins × $1,750 18 losses × $250 $41,500

Over just 30 trades with 40% win rate, accounts grow substantially. Consistency compounds.

Psychology & Trade Selection

The most important element of risk management isn't mathematical—it's psychological.

The Quality Over Quantity Mindset

Amateur traders take 50 mediocre trades per month. Professional traders take 5-10 exceptional trades. Which do you want to be?

Every trade should be a trade you're excited about—one where all stars align. If you're unsure, sit on your hands.

Key Psychological Principles

1. Accept Losses as Business Expenses

Losing trades are not failures; they're the cost of doing business. If you risk 1%, even 10 consecutive losses only cost 10% of your account. You'll recover with 2-3 winners.

2. Never Trade Emotionally

After a loss, don't revenge trade. After a big win, don't get overconfident. Stick to your process regardless of recent results.

3. Trade Your Plan, Plan Your Trade

Every trade should be planned before you enter. No impulsive decisions. Write down your entry, stop, target, and reasoning before clicking the button.

4. Don't Trade to "Make Back" Losses

The market doesn't owe you anything. Don't increase position size or take marginal setups trying to recover yesterday's loss. Trust the process.

The Trader's Oath

  • I will only take trades with 1:7 minimum R:R
  • I will never risk more than 1-2% per trade
  • I will not move stops to give trades more room
  • I will wait for all entry criteria before executing
  • I will journal every trade, win or lose
  • I will prioritize capital preservation above all else

When to Stop Trading

Set daily and weekly loss limits:

  • Daily limit: If you lose 2% of your account in one day, stop trading until tomorrow
  • Weekly limit: If you lose 5% in one week, take the rest of the week off

This prevents emotional spirals and gives you time to review what went wrong.

Warning Signs You're Trading Emotionally

  • Taking trades without full analysis
  • Increasing position size after losses
  • Checking charts obsessively
  • Feeling anxious about open positions
  • Trading outside your planned hours

If you recognize these behaviors, close your platform and step away. Come back tomorrow with a clear head.

Chapter 6

The Secret to Reading Candlestick Patterns

Candlesticks don't just show price—they reveal the battle between buyers and sellers, and who's winning.

Why Candlesticks Matter in SMC

While Smart Money Concepts focuses on structure and liquidity, candlestick patterns provide confirmation of institutional activity. A single candle can tell you:

  • Whether buyers or sellers are in control
  • If a level is being defended or violated
  • Whether a move is genuine or a trap
  • Optimal entry timing within your zone

The key is not memorizing dozens of patterns—it's understanding context.

The Most Useful Candlestick Patterns

1. Pin Bar (Hammer / Shooting Star)

A candle with a small body and long wick showing rejection.

  • Bullish Pin Bar: Long lower wick, small body at top. Shows buyers rejected lower prices
  • Bearish Pin Bar: Long upper wick, small body at bottom. Shows sellers rejected higher prices

SMC Context: When a pin bar forms at an order block or after a liquidity sweep, it's powerful confirmation of institutional defense.

2. Engulfing Candle

A candle that completely engulfs the previous candle's body.

  • Bullish Engulfing: Large green candle engulfing previous red candle. Shows momentum shift to buyers
  • Bearish Engulfing: Large red candle engulfing previous green candle. Shows momentum shift to sellers

SMC Context: After a liquidity sweep and MSS, an engulfing candle confirms institutional entry and provides your trigger.

3. Inside Bar

A candle whose high and low are completely within the previous candle's range. Shows consolidation and coiling energy.

SMC Context: Inside bars at key levels suggest institutions are accumulating before a breakout. The break direction often indicates the next move.

4. Wick Rejection

Strong wicks (without specific patterns) show aggressive defense of levels.

  • Long lower wick: Buyers defending support
  • Long upper wick: Sellers defending resistance

SMC Context: Wicks during liquidity sweeps are the footprints of stop hunts. The wick shows retail stops triggered, then institutional entry.

Context Changes Everything

A pin bar in isolation means little. But a pin bar:

  • At a 1H order block
  • After a liquidity sweep
  • Aligned with H4 bias
  • During London open

...is a high-probability setup.

The Three Questions for Every Candle

  1. Where is it forming? (at key structure, in dead zone, nowhere special?)
  2. What does the wick show? (rejection up? rejection down? indecision?)
  3. What happened before it? (liquidity sweep? MSS? nothing?)

Answer these three questions and you'll know if the candle matters.

M15 vs H1 Candle Reading

On M15 (Entry Timeframe)

Look for:

  • Sharp momentum candles after sweep (engulfing, large bodies)
  • Pin bars showing rejection back into order block
  • Candle closes beyond MSS level (confirmation)
  • Series of smaller candles followed by breakout (coiling)

On H1 (Bias Timeframe)

Look for:

  • Order blocks (last opposite-color candle before strong move)
  • Large engulfing candles showing structure breaks
  • Multiple rejection wicks at same level (strong zone)
  • Inside bar breakouts signaling direction

Combining Candlesticks with Liquidity & MSS

Here's the complete pattern:

Bullish Entry Example with Candlesticks

  1. Setup: H1 shows bullish order block at 1.2750. H4 bias is bullish.
  2. Liquidity Sweep (M15): Price drops to 1.2745 with a long wick below 1.2750, triggering retail stops.
  3. Rejection Candle (M15): Bullish pin bar forms with long lower wick and closes at 1.2758.
  4. MSS (M15): Next candle breaks above recent lower high at 1.2765.
  5. Confirmation (M15): Strong bullish engulfing candle closes at 1.2772.
  6. Entry: Enter on close of engulfing candle at 1.2772.

The combination of sweep + rejection + MSS + engulfing gives maximum confidence.

What Candles DON'T Tell You

Be careful not to over-rely on candlesticks alone:

  • They don't predict the future—only show current sentiment
  • Single candles without context are often meaningless
  • Small timeframe candles (M1, M5) are too noisy to trust
  • News events can create unusual candles that violate patterns

The Novice Mistake

New traders see a pin bar and immediately enter without checking:

  • Is this at a key level?
  • Does it align with HTF bias?
  • Was there a liquidity sweep?
  • Is there structural confluence?

Don't trade candles alone. Trade candles within the SMC framework.

Practice Exercise

Open your charts and look for:

  1. A clear H1 order block
  2. Wait for price to return to that block on M15
  3. Watch the candlestick patterns that form
  4. Note: Do you see rejection wicks? Engulfing candles? Pin bars?
  5. Did those candles lead to moves away from the block?

Do this for 10 historical examples. You'll start recognizing the patterns intuitively.

Chapter 7

Chart Patterns I Look For in Trades

Smart Money leaves footprints. Learn to recognize them and you'll always know where the institutions are positioned.

The Core SMC Patterns

These are the patterns that form the foundation of every trade:

1. Liquidity Pools

What they are: Clusters of stop losses sitting above recent highs or below recent lows.

Why they matter: Institutions need liquidity to enter large positions. They push price to these pools to trigger stops and fill their orders.

How to spot them:

  • Look for obvious swing highs in downtrends (buy-side liquidity)
  • Look for obvious swing lows in uptrends (sell-side liquidity)
  • Equal highs/lows are prime liquidity targets
  • Round numbers (1.3000, 1.2500) often hold psychological stops

Trading application: Expect price to sweep these levels before reversing. Use them as targets (if trading toward them) or entry zones (if trading the reversal from them).

2. Fair Value Gaps (FVG) / Imbalances

What they are: Three-candle patterns where the first candle's high doesn't overlap with the third candle's low (or vice versa), leaving a "gap" or imbalance.

Why they matter: Price moved so fast that it left inefficiency. The market often returns to "fill" these gaps.

How to spot them:

  • Bullish FVG: Gap between candle 1's high and candle 3's low during upward move
  • Bearish FVG: Gap between candle 1's low and candle 3's high during downward move
  • More common on M15 and H1 during volatile moves

Trading application: Mark FVGs and expect price to return to them. They often serve as entry zones or targets.

3. Order Blocks

What they are: The last opposite-colored candle before a strong impulsive move.

Why they matter: They represent the zone where institutions placed large orders that initiated the move.

How to spot them:

  • Bullish OB: Last red (bearish) candle before strong green (bullish) move up
  • Bearish OB: Last green (bullish) candle before strong red (bearish) move down
  • Works best on H1 and H4 timeframes
  • The candle should have a clear body (not just wicks)

Trading application: When price returns to an order block aligned with your bias, it's a prime entry zone. Institutions often defend these levels aggressively.

4. Inefficiencies

What they are: Any area where price moved without consolidation—gaps, long wicks, fast candles.

Why they matter: The market seeks to fill inefficiencies for balance.

How to spot them: Look for areas of vertical price movement with no lateral consolidation.

Trading application: Similar to FVGs, inefficiencies act as magnets for price to return and retest.

5. Market Structure Breaks (MSB)

What they are: Price breaking above a lower high (bullish) or below a higher low (bearish), signaling a potential trend change.

Why they matter: Confirms that momentum is shifting and institutions are repositioning.

How to spot them:

  • In downtrend: Price breaks above the most recent lower high
  • In uptrend: Price breaks below the most recent higher low
  • Must be a clear, decisive break—not just a small wick

Trading application: MSB is part of your entry confirmation. After a liquidity sweep, wait for MSB before entering.

6. Support/Resistance Flips

What they are: Previous resistance becoming support (in uptrends) or previous support becoming resistance (in downtrends).

Why they matter: Shows institutional commitment to a level. Once flipped, the level becomes a strong zone.

How to spot them:

  • Identify a level that previously acted as resistance
  • Price breaks above it decisively
  • Price returns and bounces from it (now support)

Trading application: Trade pullbacks to flipped levels aligned with overall bias.

Quick Heuristics for Prioritizing Patterns

Not all patterns are equal. Prioritize setups that have:

High-Priority Checklist

  1. H4/H1 order block + liquidity pool – Highest probability
  2. FVG within order block zone – Extra confluence
  3. Multiple patterns stacked – OB + FVG + Liquidity = best setups
  4. Fresh levels – Untested order blocks are stronger than previously tested ones
  5. Aligned with HTF bias – Pattern in direction of H4 trend always preferred

Visual Pattern Recognition Guide

Step 1: Zoom Out to H4

Identify the overall trend and major swing points. Mark the most recent swing highs and lows—these are your liquidity pools.

Step 2: Drop to H1

Within the H4 trend, find the last opposite-colored candle before a strong move. This is your order block. Also look for any fair value gaps left during fast moves.

Step 3: Switch to M15

Wait for price to return to your H1 order block or FVG. Watch for liquidity sweep below (for longs) or above (for shorts). Look for MSS confirmation.

Step 4: Execute

Once all criteria are met, enter the trade with stop beyond the sweep and target at the next liquidity pool.

Complete Pattern-to-Trade Example: GBP/USD Short

H4 Analysis (Step 1):

  • GBP/USD is in a bearish trend with lower highs and lower lows
  • Most recent swing high: 1.2850 (liquidity pool)
  • Current price: 1.2820
  • Bias: Bearish

H1 Analysis (Step 2):

  • Bearish order block identified at 1.2830-1.2840 zone
  • This was the last bullish candle before the recent drop
  • Fair value gap exists at 1.2835-1.2838
  • Target zone: 1.2830-1.2840

M15 Execution (Step 3):

  • Price rallies to 1.2852 (sweeping the 1.2850 liquidity high)
  • Strong rejection wick forms—bearish pin bar
  • Price closes back below 1.2850 at 1.2843
  • MSS occurs as price breaks below recent higher low at 1.2835
  • Bearish engulfing candle confirms at 1.2832

Trade Execution (Step 4):

  • Entry: 1.2832 (on engulfing candle close)
  • Stop Loss: 1.2862 (above liquidity sweep + buffer) = 30 pips
  • Take Profit: 1.2620 (next liquidity low) = 212 pips
  • Risk-to-Reward: 1:7.07

Result Scenario: If risking $150, potential profit is $1,060.

Common Pattern Recognition Mistakes

Avoid These Errors

  • Seeing patterns that aren't there – Don't force order blocks or FVGs. They should be obvious.
  • Ignoring HTF context – A bearish order block in a strong H4 uptrend is a trap, not a trade.
  • Trading stale levels – Order blocks lose strength after multiple tests. Fresh is best.
  • Overcomplicating – You don't need 10 patterns aligned. OB + Liquidity + MSS is enough.

Chapter 8

Key Terms You Need to Respect

Master the language, master the markets. These terms are your trading vocabulary.

Essential Smart Money Concepts Terms

Liquidity

Definition: The availability of orders to fill positions, often clustered above highs and below lows as retail stop losses.

Practical relevance: Institutions hunt liquidity before making large moves. Recognize liquidity pools to predict reversals and avoid getting stopped out prematurely.

Order Block (OB)

Definition: The last opposite-direction candle before a strong impulsive move, representing where institutions placed significant orders.

Practical relevance: Price often returns to order blocks before continuing. These zones provide high-probability entry points when aligned with bias.

Fair Value Gap (FVG)

Definition: A three-candle pattern where price leaves an imbalance or "gap" due to fast movement, with candle 1's high not touching candle 3's low (or vice versa).

Practical relevance: FVGs act as magnets—price frequently returns to fill them. Use them as target zones or entry refinement areas.

Imbalance

Definition: Any area where price moved without equilibrium, leaving inefficiency in the market.

Practical relevance: Similar to FVGs, imbalances suggest future price revisits. They help identify both entries and targets.

Market Structure Shift (MSS)

Definition: A break of the most recent higher low (in uptrends) or lower high (in downtrends), signaling potential momentum change.

Practical relevance: MSS confirms that institutional positioning is changing. It's a required element before entering trades—never enter without it.

Sweep (Liquidity Sweep)

Definition: When price temporarily pushes beyond a key level to trigger stop losses, then reverses.

Practical relevance: Sweeps reveal institutional manipulation—they hunt retail stops before entering. Recognize sweeps to enter at optimal prices rather than getting stopped out.

Wick

Definition: The thin lines above and below a candlestick body showing price rejection and the high/low of the period.

Practical relevance: Long wicks indicate strong rejection at levels. Wicks during sweeps confirm stop hunts. They show where institutions defended zones aggressively.

Critical Trading Terms

Risk-to-Reward Ratio (R:R)

Definition: The relationship between how much you risk versus how much you stand to gain on a trade.

Practical relevance: Minimum 1:7 R:R ensures long-term profitability even with win rates below 50%. Never take trades with less than this ratio.

Drawdown

Definition: The peak-to-trough decline in your account balance, usually expressed as a percentage.

Practical relevance: Drawdowns are inevitable. Proper risk management (1-2% per trade) keeps drawdowns manageable and recoverable. A 50% drawdown requires 100% gains to recover—avoid at all costs.

Stop Hunt

Definition: Deliberate price manipulation by market makers to trigger retail stop losses before reversing direction.

Practical relevance: Understanding stop hunts prevents you from placing stops in obvious areas. Place stops beyond sweep zones with structural buffer.

Confluence

Definition: When multiple technical factors align at the same level (e.g., order block + FVG + liquidity pool).

Practical relevance: Higher confluence equals higher probability setups. Look for 3+ confirming factors before entering trades.

Bias

Definition: Your directional outlook based on higher-timeframe analysis—bullish (long), bearish (short), or neutral.

Practical relevance: Having a clear daily bias prevents directionless trading. Your bias determines which setups you take and which you ignore.

Premium vs Discount Zone

Definition: Premium = upper portion of a range (expensive to buy). Discount = lower portion of a range (cheap to buy).

Practical relevance: In bullish bias, buy at discount zones. In bearish bias, sell at premium zones. Never buy at premium in uptrends or sell at discount in downtrends.

Session

Definition: Specific trading hours when major financial centers are active (London, New York, Tokyo, Sydney).

Practical relevance: Highest liquidity and best setups occur during London and New York sessions, especially their overlap. Trade quality improves significantly during these windows.

Time Frame (TF)

Definition: The chart period—M15 (15-minute), H1 (1-hour), H4 (4-hour), etc.

Practical relevance: Multiple time frames provide context. H4 for bias, H1 for zones, M15 for entries. Never trade on single time frame alone.

Position Sizing

Definition: Calculating the appropriate trade volume based on account size, risk percentage, and stop loss distance.

Practical relevance: Proper position sizing ensures you risk exactly 1-2% regardless of stop distance. Prevents overleveraging and account blow-ups.

Psychological Trading Terms

FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)

Definition: The emotional urge to enter a trade because it's moving, even though your criteria aren't met.

Practical relevance: FOMO leads to impulsive entries and losses. Remind yourself: there's always another trade. Miss the move, learn the lesson, catch the next one.

Revenge Trading

Definition: Taking trades immediately after a loss to "get back" at the market or recover losses quickly.

Practical relevance: Revenge trading is emotional, not strategic. It leads to bigger losses. After a loss, step away, analyze objectively, return with clear mind.

Overtrading

Definition: Taking too many trades, often low-quality setups, due to boredom or impatience.

Practical relevance: Quality over quantity. Taking 5 perfect setups per month beats 50 mediocre ones. More trades ≠ more profit. Better trades = more profit.

Confirmation Bias

Definition: Seeing only information that supports your existing view and ignoring contradictory evidence.

Practical relevance: Leads to ignoring warning signs and holding losing trades too long. Stay objective—if the setup deteriorates, exit regardless of your initial opinion.

Terms You Must Respect

These aren't just vocabulary—they're principles:

  • Liquidity – The market's fuel. Follow it, don't fight it.
  • Structure – The market's skeleton. Respect breaks and shifts.
  • Risk Management – Your survival mechanism. 1-2% maximum, always.
  • Patience – Your competitive advantage. Wait for perfection.
  • Discipline – Your edge. Follow the plan even when it's hard.
