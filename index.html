1. NUMBER AND NUMERATION

1.1 Number Bases

Number bases refer to different systems of counting, with decimal (base 10) being the most common.

Converting from base 10 to another base: Divide repeatedly by the new base, taking remainders
Converting from another base to base 10: Multiply each digit by its place value and sum
Example: Convert 29₁₀ to base 2
29 ÷ 2 = 14 remainder 1
14 ÷ 2 = 7 remainder 0
7 ÷ 2 = 3 remainder 1
3 ÷ 2 = 1 remainder 1
1 ÷ 2 = 0 remainder 1
Reading from bottom to top: 29₁₀ = 11101₂
When converting between bases, always work systematically and check your conversion by converting back to the original base.

1.2 Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

These are different ways of representing parts of a whole.

Percentage = Fraction × 100%
Decimal = Fraction
Fraction = Decimal = Percentage ÷ 100
Example: Express 0.75 as a fraction and a percentage
As a fraction: 0.75 = 75/100 = 3/4 (simplified)
As a percentage: 0.75 × 100% = 75%

1.3 Indices and Logarithms

Indices (powers) and logarithms are inverse operations used to express repeated multiplication.

Laws of indices:
aⁿ × aᵐ = aⁿ⁺ᵐ
aⁿ ÷ aᵐ = aⁿ⁻ᵐ
(aⁿ)ᵐ = aⁿᵐ
a⁰ = 1
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ

Logarithm: If aˣ = N, then logaN = x
Remember that logarithms are only defined for positive numbers, and the base of a logarithm must be positive and not equal to 1.

2. ALGEBRA

2.1 Algebraic Expressions

Algebraic expressions involve variables and constants combined with operations.

Like terms: Terms with identical variable parts
Factorization: Expressing a polynomial as a product of simpler expressions
Quadratic expression: ax² + bx + c, where a ≠ 0
Example: Factorize x² - 9
x² - 9 = x² - 3²
= (x + 3)(x - 3) (difference of squares formula)

2.2 Algebraic Equations

Equations are mathematical statements declaring equality between expressions.

Linear equation: ax + b = 0
Quadratic equation: ax² + bx + c = 0
Quadratic formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a
Discriminant (Δ) = b² - 4ac
For a quadratic equation:
- If Δ > 0: Two distinct real roots
- If Δ = 0: One repeated real root
- If Δ < 0: No real roots (two complex roots)

2.3 Sequences and Series

Patterns of numbers following specific rules.

Arithmetic Progression (AP):
- nth term: a + (n-1)d
- Sum of n terms: n/2[2a + (n-1)d] or n/2(first term + last term)

Geometric Progression (GP):
- nth term: ar^(n-1)
- Sum of n terms: a(1-r^n)/(1-r) for |r| < 1
- Sum of n terms: a(r^n-1)/(r-1) for |r| > 1
Example: Find the sum of the first 10 terms of the AP: 3, 7, 11, 15, ...
First term (a) = 3, Common difference (d) = 4
S₁₀ = 10/2[2(3) + (10-1)4] = 5[6 + 36] = 5[42] = 210

3. GEOMETRY AND TRIGONOMETRY

3.1 Angles and Lines

Basic building blocks of geometry dealing with directions and intersections.

Angle properties:
- Adjacent angles on a straight line sum to 180°
- Angles at a point sum to 360°
- Vertically opposite angles are equal
Example: If two parallel lines are cut by a transversal, then:
- Corresponding angles are equal
- Alternate angles are equal
- Interior angles on the same side of the transversal sum to 180°

3.2 Polygons

Closed figures with straight sides.

Sum of interior angles of a polygon with n sides = (n - 2) × 180°
Each interior angle of a regular polygon = (n - 2) × 180° / n
Each exterior angle of a regular polygon = 360° / n
Polygon Sides Sum of Interior Angles Interior Angle (Regular)
Triangle 3 180° 60°
Quadrilateral 4 360° 90°
Pentagon 5 540° 108°
Hexagon 6 720° 120°

3.3 Circle Theorems

Relationships between angles and lines in circles.

- The angle subtended by an arc at the center is twice the angle subtended at any point on the alternate segment
- Angles in the same segment are equal
- The angle in a semicircle is a right angle
- Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180°
A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of contact.

3.4 Trigonometric Ratios

Relationships between angles and sides of right-angled triangles.

sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse
cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse
tan θ = opposite / adjacent = sin θ / cos θ

Sine rule: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C
Cosine rule: c² = a² + b² - 2ab cos C
Example: In a right-angled triangle, if one angle is 30° and the hypotenuse is 10 cm, find the length of the opposite side.
sin 30° = opposite / 10
opposite = 10 × sin 30° = 10 × 0.5 = 5 cm

4. MENSURATION

4.1 Areas and Perimeters

Measurements of 2D shapes.

Rectangle: Area = length × width, Perimeter = 2(length + width)
Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height, Perimeter = sum of all sides
Circle: Area = πr², Circumference = 2πr
Parallelogram: Area = base × height
Trapezium: Area = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height

4.2 Volumes and Surface Areas

Measurements of 3D shapes.

Cube: Volume = edge³, Surface Area = 6 × edge²
Cuboid: Volume = length × width × height, Surface Area = 2(lw + lh + wh)
Cylinder: Volume = πr²h, Curved Surface Area = 2πrh, Total Surface Area = 2πr(r + h)
Sphere: Volume = (4/3)πr³, Surface Area = 4πr²
Cone: Volume = (1/3)πr²h, Curved Surface Area = πrl, Total Surface Area = πr(r + l) where l = slant height
For any prism, the volume can be calculated as the product of the base area and the height.

5. STATISTICS AND PROBABILITY

5.1 Data Representation

Methods of organizing and presenting data.

Types of data representation:
- Frequency tables
- Bar charts/histograms
- Pie charts
- Line graphs
- Scatter diagrams
When drawing a pie chart, calculate the angle for each sector using:
Angle = (Item's frequency / Total frequency) × 360°

5.2 Measures of Central Tendency

Values that represent the center or typical value of a data set.

Mean (arithmetic): Sum of all values divided by the number of values
Median: Middle value when data is arranged in order
Mode: Most frequently occurring value
Example: Find the mean, median, and mode of: 4, 7, 9, 3, 5, 9, 8
Mean = (4+7+9+3+5+9+8)/7 = 45/7 = 6.43
Median (ordered data: 3,4,5,7,8,9,9): 7
Mode: 9 (occurs twice)

5.3 Measures of Dispersion

Values that describe the spread or variation of a data set.

Range = Maximum value - Minimum value
Variance = ∑(x - μ)² / n
Standard deviation = √Variance

5.4 Probability

The measure of the likelihood of an event occurring.

P(Event) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A) = P(B) × P(A|B)
For independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
0 ≤ P(Event) ≤ 1
P(Certain event) = 1
P(Impossible event) = 0
P(Not A) = 1 - P(A)

6. VECTORS AND MATRICES

6.1 Vectors

Quantities that have both magnitude and direction.

Vector addition: v + w
Scalar multiplication: kv
Magnitude of a vector |v| = √(v₁² + v₂² + v₃²)
Unit vector: v/|v|
Dot product: v·w = |v||w|cos θ = v₁w₁ + v₂w₂ + v₃w₃
Example: Find the magnitude of v = 3i + 4j
|v| = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5

6.2 Matrices

Rectangular arrays of numbers arranged in rows and columns.

Matrix addition: (A + B)ᵢⱼ = Aᵢⱼ + Bᵢⱼ
Scalar multiplication: (kA)ᵢⱼ = kAᵢⱼ
Matrix multiplication: (AB)ᵢⱼ = ∑ Aᵢₖ × Bₖⱼ
Determinant of 2×2 matrix: |A| = ad - bc, where A = [[a, b], [c, d]]
Inverse of 2×2 matrix: A⁻¹ = 1/|A| × [[d, -b], [-c, a]], where A = [[a, b], [c, d]]
Matrix multiplication is not commutative in general: AB ≠ BA
A matrix must be square to have a determinant
A matrix has an inverse if and only if its determinant is not zero

7. CALCULUS

7.1 Differentiation

Finding the rate of change or slope of a function.

Basic rules:
- d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹
- d/dx(k) = 0 (k is constant)
- d/dx(kf(x)) = k·d/dx(f(x))
- d/dx(f(x) + g(x)) = d/dx(f(x)) + d/dx(g(x))
- d/dx(f(x) × g(x)) = f(x)·d/dx(g(x)) + g(x)·d/dx(f(x))
Example: Find the derivative of f(x) = 3x² - 2x + 5
f'(x) = 3·2x¹ - 2·1x⁰ + 0 = 6x - 2

7.2 Integration

The inverse process of differentiation, finding the area under a curve.

Basic rules:
- ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) + C (n ≠ -1)
- ∫k dx = kx + C (k is constant)
- ∫(f(x) + g(x)) dx = ∫f(x) dx + ∫g(x) dx
- ∫k·f(x) dx = k·∫f(x) dx
Definite integral: ∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a), where F is the antiderivative of f
Example: Evaluate ∫02 (3x² + 2) dx
∫(3x² + 2) dx = 3x³/3 + 2x + C = x³ + 2x + C
∫02 (3x² + 2) dx = (2³ + 2·2) - (0³ + 2·0) = 8 + 4 - 0 = 12

8. EXAMINATION TIPS

8.1 General Tips

  • Read each question carefully, identifying what is given and what is required.
  • Show all your working clearly and systematically.
  • Manage your time wisely, spending more time on higher-mark questions.
  • Check your answers if time permits.
  • Ensure your calculator is set to the correct mode (degrees/radians) for trigonometric calculations.

8.2 Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to include units in your final answer.
  • Computational errors in basic arithmetic.
  • Not reading the question carefully and missing key information.
  • Forgetting to check if your answer makes sense in the context of the problem.
  • Incorrect application of formulas.

8.3 Last-Minute Preparation

  • Review key formulas, properties, and theorems.
  • Practice with past WAEC questions to get familiar with the format and style.
  • Focus on areas where you have challenges.
  • Ensure you have all required materials (calculator, mathematical set, etc.) ready before the exam day.
  • Get adequate rest before the examination.
