ENGLISH LANGUAGE COMPLETE STUDY GUIDE

Comprehensive Preparation for WAEC English Language Examination

EXAMINATION OBJECTIVES

The WAEC English Language examination aims to test candidates' ability to:

  • Comply with the rules of grammar, spelling and punctuation
  • Comprehend written and spoken English
  • Recognize implied meaning, tones and attitudes
  • Use an acceptable pronunciation that can be comprehended by others
  • Isolate and summarize relevant information from set passages
  • Communicate effectively in writing
  • Express ideas clearly, correctly and appropriately

1. LEXIS AND STRUCTURE

1.1 Grammar Rules

English grammar consists of rules for constructing sentences and communicating effectively.

Parts of Speech:
- Nouns: names of people, places, things, or ideas
- Verbs: action or state words
- Adjectives: words that describe nouns
- Adverbs: words that modify verbs, adjectives, or other adverbs
- Pronouns: words that replace nouns
- Prepositions: words that show relationships between other words
- Conjunctions: words that connect words, phrases, or clauses
- Interjections: words expressing emotion
Example: Identifying parts of speech
"The girl (noun) quickly (adverb) solved (verb) the difficult (adjective) puzzle."
"She (pronoun) walked through (preposition) the park and (conjunction) met her friends. Wow! (interjection) It was fun."
Always identify the subject and verb in a sentence first, as they form the foundation of English sentence structure.

1.2 Tenses

Tenses indicate when an action takes place: past, present, or future.

Main Tense Groups:
- Simple: I walk / I walked / I will walk
- Continuous: I am walking / I was walking / I will be walking
- Perfect: I have walked / I had walked / I will have walked
- Perfect Continuous: I have been walking / I had been walking / I will have been walking
Example: Simple Present vs. Present Continuous
Simple Present (habitual action): "He plays football every Sunday."
Present Continuous (action in progress): "He is playing football right now."
Example: Past Perfect vs. Simple Past
Past Perfect (action completed before another past action): "I had finished my homework before dinner."
Simple Past (completed action): "I finished my homework yesterday."

1.3 Punctuation

Punctuation marks help clarify meaning and organize written text.

Common punctuation marks:
- Period (.): ends a declarative sentence
- Comma (,): separates elements in a list or clauses
- Question mark (?): ends an interrogative sentence
- Exclamation point (!): indicates strong emotion
- Colon (:): introduces a list or explanation
- Semicolon (;): connects related independent clauses
- Apostrophe ('): shows possession or contraction
- Quotation marks (""): indicate direct speech or quotes
Remember that punctuation can completely change the meaning of a sentence. Compare: "Let's eat, Grandma!" versus "Let's eat Grandma!"

1.4 Common Grammatical Errors

Avoiding these common errors will improve your writing quality.

Common errors to avoid:
- Subject-verb agreement: "The group of students is (not are) here."
- Pronoun-antecedent agreement: "Each student must bring his or her (not their) book."
- Misplaced modifiers: "Walking down the street, the trees were beautiful" should be "Walking down the street, I saw beautiful trees"
- Dangling participles: "Having finished the assignment, the TV was turned on" should be "Having finished the assignment, I turned on the TV"

Exam Tips: Lexis and Structure

In the objective test section (Paper 2):

  • Questions often test your ability to identify grammatical errors in sentences
  • You may need to select the correct form of a word from multiple options
  • Practice identifying parts of speech and their correct usage
  • Be familiar with common idioms and expressions
  • Study verb forms and tense consistency carefully

2. COMPREHENSION AND SUMMARY

2.1 Reading Comprehension

Reading comprehension involves understanding, interpreting, and analyzing written text. It requires both literal and inferential understanding of the content presented in a passage.

Key reading comprehension skills include identifying the main idea, recognizing supporting details, making inferences, understanding vocabulary in context, and identifying the author's purpose and tone.

When approaching a comprehension passage in the WAEC examination, it's recommended to follow these steps:

  1. Skim the passage quickly to get a general idea of the topic
  2. Read the questions to know what information to look for
  3. Read the passage carefully, making mental notes of key points
  4. Answer questions by referring back to specific parts of the text
  5. For vocabulary questions, pay attention to context clues that help determine meaning
  6. For inference questions, look for implied information rather than directly stated facts
Types of comprehension questions:
- Literal: answers directly stated in the text
- Inferential: requires reading between the lines
- Evaluative: requires judgment based on text and personal knowledge
- Vocabulary: understanding words in context
- Main idea: identifying central theme or purpose
- Detail: identifying specific information
Example: Types of Questions
Passage excerpt: "Despite numerous challenges, Adeola persevered in her studies. Working two jobs and caring for her siblings, she still managed to graduate with honors."

Literal question: What did Adeola achieve despite her challenges?
Inferential question: What quality does Adeola demonstrate in this passage?
Evaluative question: Do you think Adeola's achievement is remarkable? Why or why not?

2.2 Summary Writing

Summary writing tests your ability to identify main points and condense information without losing essential meaning.

Steps for effective summary writing:
1. Read the passage carefully to understand the main ideas
2. Identify key points and supporting details
3. Discard irrelevant information and examples
4. Rewrite the main points in your own words
5. Ensure your summary is concise (usually about one-third of the original)
6. Maintain the author's intended meaning
7. Use appropriate linking words for coherence
In summary writing, focus on content points rather than examples or illustrations. Use your own words as much as possible while maintaining accuracy.

Exam Tips: Comprehension and Summary

  • Practice reading various types of passages: narrative, descriptive, expository, argumentative
  • Develop your vocabulary to better understand texts
  • Train yourself to identify main ideas quickly
  • Practice answering different types of comprehension questions
  • For summary writing, stick to the word limit and focus on main points
  • Avoid copying sentences directly from the passage

Practice Exercise

Directions: Read the following passage and practice identifying the main ideas and supporting details. Then, write a summary of about 100 words.

Environmental pollution is one of the biggest challenges facing humanity today. Air pollution from factories and vehicles releases harmful gases into the atmosphere, contributing to respiratory diseases and climate change. Water pollution from industrial waste and agricultural runoff contaminates our rivers and oceans, harming aquatic life and affecting drinking water sources. Land pollution from improper waste disposal destroys habitats and leaches toxins into the soil. To address these issues, governments worldwide are implementing stricter regulations on emissions and waste disposal. Individuals can also contribute by reducing their carbon footprint, recycling, and properly disposing of waste. Educational institutions are raising awareness about environmental conservation. Only through collaborative efforts can we hope to create a cleaner, healthier planet for future generations.

3. ESSAY WRITING

3.1 Types of Essays

WAEC English Language examination may include various types of essays:

- Narrative: telling a story
- Descriptive: detailed portrayal of a person, place, or thing
- Expository: explaining a topic
- Argumentative: presenting an argument with evidence
- Letters/Speeches: formal or informal communication
Example: Essay question types
Narrative: "Write a story that ends with the statement: '...and that was how I learned to be careful with strangers.'"
Descriptive: "Describe a traditional festival in your community."
Expository: "Explain the causes and effects of unemployment in your country."
Argumentative: "The use of mobile phones should be allowed in schools. Do you agree?"
Letter: "Write a letter to your local government chairman about the poor state of roads in your area."

3.2 Essay Structure

A well-structured essay typically follows this format:

Introduction:
- Hook/attention grabber
- Background information
- Thesis statement/main idea

Body paragraphs (2-3):
- Topic sentence
- Supporting details/evidence
- Examples
- Explanation
- Transition to next paragraph

Conclusion:
- Restatement of main idea
- Summary of key points
- Final thought or call to action
Always plan your essay before writing. Spend about 5 minutes outlining your main points and organizing your thoughts. This will make your essay more coherent and well-structured.

3.3 Writing Tips

Follow these guidelines to improve your essay writing:

Language use:
- Use varied vocabulary and sentence structures
- Avoid slang and colloquial expressions
- Use appropriate transitional words and phrases
- Maintain consistent tense

Content:
- Address all aspects of the question
- Provide relevant examples and illustrations
- Develop ideas logically and clearly
- Show originality and creativity

Technical aspects:
- Pay attention to grammar, spelling, and punctuation
- Write legibly
- Paragraphing should be clear and logical
- Stay within the word limit (typically 450-500 words)
In the WAEC examination, your essay is assessed on content, organization, expression, and mechanical accuracy. Pay attention to all these aspects.

Exam Tips: Essay Writing

  • Read the instructions carefully and choose the essay type you're most comfortable with
  • Spend time planning before writing
  • Write a clear introduction that states your main points
  • Develop each paragraph around one main idea
  • Use relevant examples to support your points
  • Conclude effectively, summarizing your main arguments
  • Proofread your work for grammatical and spelling errors
  • Aim for about 450-500 words (approximately 3 pages)

4. ORAL ENGLISH

4.1 Sound System

Oral English tests your knowledge of English pronunciation and sound patterns.

Vowel sounds:
- Short vowels: /ɪ/ (sit), /e/ (bed), /æ/ (cat), /ʌ/ (cup), /ʊ/ (book), /ɒ/ (hot), /ə/ (about)
- Long vowels: /iː/ (seat), /ɜː/ (bird), /ɑː/ (car), /ɔː/ (saw), /uː/ (boot)
- Diphthongs: /eɪ/ (day), /aɪ/ (my), /ɔɪ/ (boy), /əʊ/ (no), /aʊ/ (now), /ɪə/ (near), /eə/ (hair), /ʊə/ (pure)

Consonant sounds:
- Plosives: /p/ (pen), /b/ (bad), /t/ (tea), /d/ (dog), /k/ (cat), /g/ (go)
- Fricatives: /f/ (fat), /v/ (van), /θ/ (thin), /ð/ (this), /s/ (so), /z/ (zoo), /ʃ/ (she), /ʒ/ (measure), /h/ (hat)
- Affricates: /tʃ/ (chair), /dʒ/ (jam)
- Nasals: /m/ (man), /n/ (no), /ŋ/ (sing)
- Laterals: /l/ (leg)
- Approximants: /r/ (red), /j/ (yes), /w/ (wet)

4.2 Word Stress and Intonation

Stress and intonation patterns affect meaning in English.

Word stress rules:
- Most two-syllable nouns and adjectives: stress on first syllable (TA-ble, HAP-py)
- Most two-syllable verbs: stress on second syllable (a-GREE, de-CIDE)
- Words ending in -tion, -sion, -cian: stress on syllable before ending (e-du-CA-tion)
- Compound nouns: stress on first word (BLACK-board)

Intonation patterns:
- Falling intonation: used for statements, commands, wh-questions
- Rising intonation: used for yes/no questions, requests
- Fall-rise intonation: used for uncertainty, contrast
Example: Word stress changes meaning
CON-duct (noun): behavior
con-DUCT (verb): to lead or direct

PRE-sent (noun): gift
pre-SENT (verb): to give or show

Exam Tips: Oral English

  • Practice listening to native English speakers to familiarize yourself with correct pronunciation
  • Learn to identify sound contrasts (e.g., /p/ vs. /b/, /s/ vs. /z/)
  • Study common word stress patterns
  • Understand how intonation affects meaning
  • Practice recognizing rhyming words and syllable counts
  • Learn to identify homophones (words that sound the same but have different meanings)

5. SCHEME OF EXAMINATION

5.1 Paper Structure

The WAEC English Language examination consists of three papers:

Paper 1: Essay Writing, Comprehension and Summary (2½ hours)
- Section A: Essay writing (50 marks)
- Section B: Comprehension (20 marks)
- Section C: Summary (30 marks)

Paper 2: Objective Test - Lexis, Structure, and Literature (1 hour)
- 80 multiple-choice questions (1 mark each)

Paper 3: Oral English Test (45 minutes)
- 60 multiple-choice questions on pronunciation, stress, and intonation (1 mark each)

5.2 Marks Distribution

Total marks for the three papers:

- 220 marks for The Gambia and Sierra Leone
- 200 marks for Nigeria
The highest weightage is given to Paper 1, especially the essay writing component. Focus on developing strong writing skills.

6. EXAMINATION PREPARATION TIPS

6.1 Study Strategies

Effective preparation involves consistent practice and targeted study.

Long-term preparation:
- Read widely (newspapers, novels, articles) to improve vocabulary and comprehension
- Practice writing essays regularly on various topics
- Keep a vocabulary journal for new words
- Study grammar rules and practice applying them
- Listen to English speakers (radio, TV, podcasts) to improve oral skills

Short-term preparation:
- Review past WAEC papers and practice with them
- Focus on weak areas identified during practice
- Study common essay topics and prepare outlines
- Memorize important grammar rules and exceptions
- Practice time management during mock exams

6.2 Time Management

Effective time allocation during the examination is crucial for success.

Paper 1 (2½ hours):
- Essay writing: 1 hour
- Comprehension: 45 minutes
- Summary: 45 minutes

Paper 2 (1 hour):
- About 45 seconds per question

Paper 3 (45 minutes):
- About 45 seconds per question
If you get stuck on a question, especially in the objective papers, move on and return to it later if time permits. Don't waste precious time on difficult questions.
