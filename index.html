🔼 Bullish Continuation Patterns

These patterns suggest the uptrend will continue after a brief consolidation period.

BULLISH

1. Bullish Flag

A sharp upward move (flagpole) followed by a rectangular consolidation that slopes slightly downward. Breakout continues the uptrend with similar magnitude to the flagpole.

BULLISH

2. Bullish Pennant

Similar to a flag but with converging trendlines forming a small symmetrical triangle after a strong upward move. Brief pause before continuation.

BULLISH

3. Ascending Triangle

Horizontal resistance line with rising support line. Indicates buyers becoming more aggressive with each dip, typically breaks upward.

BULLISH

4. Symmetrical Triangle (Upward)

Converging trendlines with equal slopes. In uptrend context, typically continues upward after consolidation. Volume decreases during formation.

BULLISH

5. Cup with Handle

U-shaped rounded bottom (cup) followed by a small downward drift (handle). Classic bullish continuation pattern popularized by William O'Neil.

BULLISH

6. Measured Move Up

Three distinct moves: initial rally, correction/consolidation, then continuation rally of similar magnitude to first move. Predictable target.

BULLISH

7. Ascending Scallop

Series of rounded bottoms that gradually ascend, showing increasing buying pressure over time. Each bottom higher than previous.

BULLISH

8. Rectangle (Bullish)

Price moves between parallel support and resistance. In uptrend, typically breaks upward to continue trend. Also called trading range.

BULLISH

9. Three Rising Valleys

Three consecutive lows that form at progressively higher levels, showing strong support building and sellers losing control.

BULLISH

10. Bullish Channel (Parallel Up)

Two parallel trendlines sloping upward. Price respects both support and resistance within channel, bouncing between boundaries.

🔽 Bearish Continuation Patterns

These patterns suggest the downtrend will continue after a temporary consolidation period.

BEARISH

1. Bearish Flag

Sharp downward move (flagpole) followed by rectangular consolidation sloping slightly upward before continuing down with similar magnitude.

BEARISH

2. Bearish Pennant

Strong downward move followed by small converging triangle before breakdown continues the downtrend. Short-term consolidation pattern.

BEARISH

3. Descending Triangle

Horizontal support line with descending resistance. Shows sellers becoming more aggressive with each bounce, typically breaks downward.

BEARISH

4. Symmetrical Triangle (Downward)

Converging trendlines in downtrend context. Consolidation typically resolves with downward continuation. Decreasing volume during formation.

BEARISH

5. Inverted Cup with Handle

Inverted U-shape (cup) followed by small upward drift (handle). Bearish continuation after breakdown through support.

BEARISH

6. Measured Move Down

Initial decline, consolidation/correction, then continuation decline of similar magnitude to first move down. Provides price target.

BEARISH

7. Descending Scallop

Series of rounded tops that gradually descend, showing increasing selling pressure over time. Each top lower than previous.

BEARISH

8. Rectangle (Bearish)

Horizontal consolidation within downtrend. Typically breaks down to continue the bearish move after distribution phase.

BEARISH

9. Three Descending Peaks

Three consecutive highs forming at progressively lower levels, showing weakening resistance and buyers losing control.

BEARISH

10. Bearish Channel (Parallel Down)

Two parallel trendlines sloping downward. Price respects channel boundaries during downtrend, bouncing between support and resistance.

🔄 Bullish Reversal Patterns

These patterns signal a potential change from downtrend to uptrend.

REVERSAL

1. Double Bottom

Two distinct lows at approximately same level with moderate peak between. Classic reversal signaling strong support. Confirmed on breakout above middle peak.

REVERSAL

2. Triple Bottom

Three lows at similar level with rallies between. Stronger reversal signal than double bottom showing sellers exhausted after three failed attempts.

REVERSAL

3. Inverted Head and Shoulders

Three lows with middle low (head) deeper than two shoulders. Very reliable bullish reversal pattern. Neckline breakout confirms reversal.

REVERSAL

4. Rounding Bottom (Saucer)

Smooth U-shaped curve forming over extended period. Gradual shift from bearish to bullish sentiment. Long-term accumulation pattern.

REVERSAL

5. Diamond Bottom

Rare pattern forming diamond shape at market bottom. Price range expands then contracts before upward breakout. High volatility pattern.

REVERSAL

6. Falling Wedge (Bullish)

Converging downward-sloping trendlines. Decreasing volume and range indicate selling exhaustion before bullish breakout reversal.

REVERSAL

7. Descending Broadening Wedge

Expanding price range with downward bias. Increased volatility and wider swings often precede bullish breakout from bottom.

REVERSAL

8. Bump and Run Reversal (Bottom)

Sharp decline (bump) followed by consolidation along support trendline before explosive upward run begins. Capitulation reversal.

REVERSAL

9. Adam and Eve Double Bottom

Sharp V-bottom (Adam) followed by rounded bottom (Eve). Combination pattern showing initial panic then gradual accumulation for strong reversal.

REVERSAL

10. Complex Head and Shoulders Bottom

Multiple shoulders or heads at similar levels. More complex formation but still reliable bullish reversal. Takes longer to develop.

🔄 Bearish Reversal Patterns

These patterns signal a potential change from uptrend to downtrend.

REVERSAL

1. Double Top

Two peaks at approximately same level with moderate trough between. Classic resistance rejection pattern. Breakdown below trough confirms reversal.

REVERSAL

2. Triple Top

Three peaks at similar level with declines between. Strong resistance showing distribution phase. Buyers exhausted after three failed attempts.

REVERSAL

3. Head and Shoulders (Top)

Three peaks with middle peak (head) higher than two shoulders. Most reliable bearish reversal pattern. Neckline breakdown confirms trend change.

REVERSAL

4. Rounding Top (Saucer Top)

Inverted U-shaped curve forming over extended time. Gradual shift from bullish to bearish sentiment. Long-term distribution pattern.

REVERSAL

5. Diamond Top

Rare diamond-shaped pattern at market top. Price range expands then contracts before downward breakdown. High volatility exhaustion.

REVERSAL

6. Rising Wedge (Bearish)

Converging upward-sloping trendlines. Momentum slowing despite higher prices signals buying exhaustion and impending reversal.

REVERSAL

7. Ascending Broadening Wedge

Expanding price range with upward bias. Increasing volatility and wider swings often precede bearish breakdown from top.

REVERSAL

8. Bump and Run Reversal (Top)

Sharp rally (bump) followed by consolidation along resistance trendline before downward run begins. Climax top reversal.

REVERSAL

9. Adam and Eve Double Top

Sharp peak (Adam) followed by rounded top (Eve). Combination showing initial spike then gradual distribution for strong bearish reversal.

REVERSAL

10. Complex Head and Shoulders Top

Multiple shoulders or heads at similar levels. Complex but reliable bearish reversal formation. Takes longer time to complete.

⚖️ Neutral / Bilateral Patterns

These patterns can break out in either direction depending on market conditions and volume.

NEUTRAL

1. Symmetrical Triangle

Converging trendlines with equal slopes creating triangle. Can break either direction, typically continues prevailing trend. Volume contracts during formation.

NEUTRAL

2. Rectangle (Neutral)

Horizontal consolidation between parallel support and resistance. Breakout direction with volume determines next significant move.

NEUTRAL

3. Broadening Formation (Symmetrical)

Expanding price range with diverging trendlines. Increasing volatility signals market indecision and uncertainty about direction.

NEUTRAL

4. Megaphone Pattern

Similar to broadening formation with expanding swings. Shows extreme market uncertainty and emotional trading. High risk pattern.

NEUTRAL

5. Diamond Formation

Price expands then contracts forming diamond shape. Can break either direction from apex. Rare but significant when found.

NEUTRAL

6. Consolidation Zone

Sideways price action without clear defined pattern. Represents accumulation or distribution phase before next directional move.

🎵 Harmonic Patterns

Advanced patterns based on Fibonacci ratios and geometric price movements. Require precise measurement.

HARMONIC

1. Gartley Pattern (Bullish/Bearish)

M or W shape with specific Fibonacci ratios (0.618 XA retracement). Most common harmonic pattern. Bullish or bearish reversal signal.

HARMONIC

2. Bat Pattern

Similar to Gartley but with deeper 0.886 retracement of XA leg. Point D extends to 1.618-2.618 BC. Strong reversal indicator.

HARMONIC

3. Butterfly Pattern

Extension pattern with D point beyond X. Uses 1.27 or 1.618 extension of XA for potential reversal zone. Aggressive entry pattern.

HARMONIC

4. Crab Pattern

Extreme harmonic pattern with 1.618 extension of XA. Deepest retracement pattern (3.14-3.618 BC extension). Highest accuracy rate.

HARMONIC

5. Shark Pattern

Unique pattern with 0.886 and 1.13 ratios at point C. Provides earlier reversal signal than other harmonics. Less common formation.

HARMONIC

6. Cypher Pattern

Modern harmonic using 0.786 XA retracement and 1.272-1.414 XC projection. Less common but reliable when identified correctly.

🌊 Wave and Fractal Patterns

Complex patterns based on wave theory and market psychology cycles. Used for long-term analysis.

WAVE

1. Elliott Wave

Five-wave impulse (1,2,3,4,5) followed by three-wave correction (A,B,C). Foundation of wave analysis theory. Fractal nature applies across timeframes.

WAVE

2. Wolfe Wave

Natural rhythm pattern with converging wedge lines and five points. Predicts both price target and timing. Based on supply-demand equilibrium.

WAVE

3. ABCD Pattern

Simple three-move pattern (AB, BC, CD) with equal legs. BC retraces 0.618 of AB, CD extends 1.272-1.618 of BC. Foundation for harmonics.

🕯️ Candlestick-Based Patterns

Short-term patterns formed by one to three candlesticks, showing immediate sentiment shifts and reversal signals.

CANDLESTICK

1. Morning Star / Evening Star

Three-candle reversal: downtrend/uptrend candle, small-body star showing indecision, then strong reversal candle. Morning star bullish, evening star bearish.

CANDLESTICK

2. Engulfing Pattern

Second candle completely engulfs first candle's body. Bullish engulfing at bottom signals reversal up, bearish at top signals down. Strong momentum shift.

CANDLESTICK

3. Doji Reversal

Open and close at same level creating cross or plus sign. Shows indecision and potential reversal. Most significant at trend extremes.

CANDLESTICK

4. Three White Soldiers / Three Black Crows

Three consecutive strong same-direction candles with progressively higher/lower closes. White soldiers (bullish) or black crows (bearish) show strong momentum.

CANDLESTICK

5. Hammer / Hanging Man

Small body with long lower shadow (2-3x body length). Hammer (bullish) at bottom shows rejection of lower prices, hanging man (bearish) at top warns of reversal.

CANDLESTICK

6. Shooting Star / Inverted Hammer

Small body with long upper shadow. Shooting star (bearish) at top shows rejection of higher prices, inverted hammer (bullish) at bottom signals potential reversal.

📖 Conclusion & Trading Tips

Mastering chart patterns is essential for successful technical analysis. These patterns represent the collective psychology of market participants and provide valuable insights into potential price movements. Understanding these formations gives you a significant edge in identifying high-probability trading opportunities.

🎯 Key Principles to Remember:

  • ✓ No pattern is 100% accurate - Always use confirmation signals and multiple timeframes
  • ✓ Volume is crucial - Breakouts with high volume are more reliable than low-volume moves
  • ✓ Context matters - Consider the broader market trend and where pattern forms
  • ✓ Practice identification - Review historical charts daily to train your pattern recognition
  • ✓ Risk management is paramount - Always use stop-losses and proper position sizing
  • ✓ Higher timeframes are more reliable - Daily and weekly patterns have better success rates
  • ✓ Combine with other indicators - Use RSI, MACD, moving averages for confirmation
  • ✓ Patience pays off - Wait for complete pattern formation before entering trades

📊 Pattern Success Tips:

Continuation Patterns: Best traded in strong trends. Enter on breakout with volume confirmation.

Reversal Patterns: Wait for complete formation and neckline/support break. More reliable at major support/resistance.

Harmonic Patterns: Require precise Fibonacci measurements. Use for reversal trades at potential reversal zones (PRZ).

Candlestick Patterns: Best for short-term trades. Confirm with follow-through price action next day.
