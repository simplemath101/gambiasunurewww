MATHEMATICS KEY POINTS

Comprehensive Study Guide for WAEC Mathematics Examination

1. NUMBER AND NUMERATION

Numbers are the foundation of mathematics and are used everywhere in daily life - from counting money to measuring ingredients when cooking. Let's explore different ways we can represent and work with numbers!

1.1 Number Bases

Imagine you're from a planet where people have only 8 fingers instead of 10. You'd probably count differently! That's what number bases are about - different systems of counting. We humans typically use base 10 (decimal) because we have 10 fingers, but computers use base 2 (binary) because they work with electrical switches that are either ON or OFF.

Converting from base 10 to another base: Think of it as repeatedly dividing and keeping track of remainders
Converting from another base to base 10: Each digit's value depends on its position, just like how 352 means 3 hundreds + 5 tens + 2 ones
Example: Convert 29₁₀ to base 2 (binary)
29 ÷ 2 = 14 remainder 1
14 ÷ 2 = 7 remainder 0
7 ÷ 2 = 3 remainder 1
3 ÷ 2 = 1 remainder 1
1 ÷ 2 = 0 remainder 1
Reading from bottom to top: 29₁₀ = 11101₂
Real-world application: Computer scientists and programmers work with binary (base 2), octal (base 8), and hexadecimal (base 16) daily. Digital storage, memory addresses, and color codes in web design all use different number bases!
Think of converting between bases like translating between languages - the value stays the same, but the representation changes. Always check your conversion by converting back to make sure you get the original number.

1.2 Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

These are three different ways to express parts of a whole - like three different languages saying the same thing. Imagine cutting a pizza: you could say you ate 3/4 of it (fraction), 0.75 of it (decimal), or 75% of it (percentage).

Percentage = Fraction × 100%
Decimal = Fraction
Fraction = Decimal = Percentage ÷ 100
Example: Express 0.75 as a fraction and a percentage
As a fraction: 0.75 = 75/100 = 3/4 (simplified)
As a percentage: 0.75 × 100% = 75%
Real-world application: Discounts in shops (20% off), test scores (you got 90/100 or 0.9 or 90%), phone battery level (75% charged), and cooking measurements all use these interchangeably.

1.3 Indices and Logarithms

Indices (also called exponents or powers) are a shorthand way of writing repeated multiplication. Instead of writing 2×2×2×2, we can write 2⁴. Logarithms are the reverse - they tell you what power you need to raise a number to get another number.

Laws of indices:
aⁿ × aᵐ = aⁿ⁺ᵐ (When multiplying same bases, add the powers)
aⁿ ÷ aᵐ = aⁿ⁻ᵐ (When dividing same bases, subtract the powers)
(aⁿ)ᵐ = aⁿᵐ (Power of a power? Multiply the powers)
a⁰ = 1 (Anything to the power zero equals 1)
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ (Negative power means put it in the denominator)

Logarithm: If aˣ = N, then logaN = x (log asks "a to what power gives N?")
Example: Simplify 2³ × 2⁵
Using the first law of indices: 2³ × 2⁵ = 2³⁺⁵ = 2⁸ = 256
Real-world application: Compound interest in banking uses exponential growth. The Richter scale for measuring earthquakes and the pH scale in chemistry are both logarithmic scales. Computer science uses logarithms for efficient algorithms.
Remember that logarithms only work with positive numbers (you can't take the log of a negative number or zero), and the base of a logarithm must be positive and not equal to 1.

2. ALGEBRA

Algebra is like the language of mathematics that allows us to describe patterns, relationships, and solve problems with unknowns. Think of variables (like x and y) as containers that can hold different values.

2.1 Algebraic Expressions

Algebraic expressions are combinations of variables, numbers, and operations. Just as we can simplify "4 apples + 3 apples" to "7 apples," we can simplify "4x + 3x" to "7x".

Like terms: Terms with identical variable parts (like 3x and 5x, but not 3x and 5y)
Factorization: Finding what multiplies together to make an expression (like finding that 12 = 3 × 4)
Quadratic expression: ax² + bx + c, where a ≠ 0 (has an x² term)
Example: Factorize x² - 9
x² - 9 = x² - 3²
= (x + 3)(x - 3) (difference of squares formula: a² - b² = (a+b)(a-b))
Real-world application: Architects use algebraic expressions to calculate dimensions and areas. Businesses use them for cost and profit models. Scientists use them to describe physical relationships like distance = speed × time.

2.2 Algebraic Equations

An equation is like a balance scale - whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other to keep it balanced. Solving equations means finding what value(s) make the equation true.

Linear equation: ax + b = 0 (has a highest power of 1)
Quadratic equation: ax² + bx + c = 0 (has a highest power of 2)
Quadratic formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a (the master key for solving any quadratic equation)
Discriminant (Δ) = b² - 4ac (tells you how many solutions the equation has)
Example: Solve x² - 5x + 6 = 0
Using the quadratic formula with a=1, b=-5, c=6:
x = (5 ± √(25 - 24)) / 2
x = (5 ± √1) / 2
x = (5 ± 1) / 2
x = 3 or x = 2
For a quadratic equation, the discriminant (Δ = b² - 4ac) tells you a lot:
- If Δ > 0: Two different real roots (the equation crosses the x-axis twice)
- If Δ = 0: One repeated real root (the equation touches the x-axis once)
- If Δ < 0: No real roots (the equation never crosses the x-axis)
Real-world application: Engineers use equations to design bridges and buildings. Physicists use them to describe motion. Economists use them to model financial trends.

2.3 Sequences and Series

Sequences are patterns of numbers that follow a rule, like steps on a staircase. Series are the sum of sequence terms, like adding up all those steps.

Arithmetic Progression (AP): Numbers increase by adding a fixed amount (like 3, 7, 11, 15...)
- nth term: a + (n-1)d (first term + (position - 1) × difference)
- Sum of n terms: n/2[2a + (n-1)d] or n/2(first term + last term)

Geometric Progression (GP): Numbers increase by multiplying by a fixed amount (like 2, 6, 18, 54...)
- nth term: ar^(n-1) (first term × ratio^(position-1))
- Sum of n terms: a(1-r^n)/(1-r) for |r| < 1
- Sum of n terms: a(r^n-1)/(r-1) for |r| > 1
Example: Find the sum of the first 10 terms of the AP: 3, 7, 11, 15, ...
First term (a) = 3, Common difference (d) = 4
S₁₀ = 10/2[2(3) + (10-1)4] = 5[6 + 36] = 5[42] = 210
Real-world application: Monthly savings plans form arithmetic sequences. Population growth and compound interest form geometric sequences. The Fibonacci sequence (where each number is the sum of the two before it) appears in nature, from sunflower seed patterns to pine cone spirals.

3. GEOMETRY AND TRIGONOMETRY

Geometry is the study of shapes and spaces around us. Trigonometry specifically deals with relationships between angles and sides of triangles. Together, they help us understand and measure the physical world.

3.1 Angles and Lines

Angles and lines are the building blocks of all shapes. Understanding their properties helps us analyze more complex geometric figures.

Angle properties:
- Adjacent angles on a straight line sum to 180° (like cutting a straight line into two pieces)
- Angles at a point sum to 360° (like slicing a pizza)
- Vertically opposite angles are equal (when two lines cross)
Example: When parallel lines are cut by another line (transversal):
- Corresponding angles are equal (like F-shaped angles)
- Alternate angles are equal (like Z-shaped angles)
- Interior angles on the same side sum to 180° (like C-shaped angles)
[Imagine two horizontal parallel lines with a diagonal line crossing through both. The angles formed follow these patterns.]
Real-world application: Road intersections use angle principles. Carpenters use angle relationships to ensure structures are properly aligned. Map navigation relies on understanding angles and directions.

3.2 Polygons

Polygons are closed shapes with straight sides - from triangles to hexagons and beyond. They're everywhere in the world around us, from floor tiles to stop signs.

Sum of interior angles of a polygon with n sides = (n - 2) × 180°
Each interior angle of a regular polygon = (n - 2) × 180° / n
Each exterior angle of a regular polygon = 360° / n
Polygon Sides Sum of Interior Angles Interior Angle (Regular) Real-world Example
Triangle 3 180° 60° Yield sign, pizza slice
Quadrilateral 4 360° 90° Picture frame, book cover
Pentagon 5 540° 108° US Pentagon building, home plate in baseball
Hexagon 6 720° 120° Honeycomb cells, nuts and bolts
You can verify the formula for sum of interior angles by drawing any polygon and dividing it into triangles from one vertex. Since each triangle has 180°, and you can make (n-2) triangles, the total is (n-2) × 180°.

3.3 Circle Theorems

Circles have special properties that relate angles, arcs, and lines. These relationships are incredibly useful in engineering, architecture, and design.

- The angle at the center is twice the angle at the circumference (looking at the same arc)
- Angles in the same segment are equal (like all angles standing on the same arc)
- The angle in a semicircle is a right angle (90°)
- Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180° (supplementary)
A tangent to a circle is perpendicular (90°) to the radius at the point of contact. This is why bicycle spokes are perpendicular to the rim at their connection points!
Real-world application: Gears and wheels use circle principles. Satellite dishes are designed using circular properties. GPS systems use circular geometry to determine location.

3.4 Trigonometric Ratios

Trigonometry helps us relate angles to distances. Think of it as a toolkit for solving problems involving triangles, especially right-angled triangles.

In a right-angled triangle:
sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse (remember: SOH)
cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse (remember: CAH)
tan θ = opposite / adjacent = sin θ / cos θ (remember: TOA)

For any triangle:
Sine rule: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C (sides proportional to sines of opposite angles)
Cosine rule: c² = a² + b² - 2ab cos C (generalized Pythagorean theorem)
Example: In a right-angled triangle, if one angle is 30° and the hypotenuse is 10 cm, find the length of the opposite side.
sin 30° = opposite / 10
opposite = 10 × sin 30° = 10 × 0.5 = 5 cm
Real-world application: Surveyors use trigonometry to measure land. Navigators use it to determine distances and directions. Architects use it for calculating heights and angles in buildings. Even sound waves and electrical signals are analyzed using trigonometric functions!

4. MENSURATION

Mensuration is all about measuring geometric figures - their lengths, areas, volumes, and more. It's the mathematics of measurement that we use daily, from wrapping a gift to constructing a building.

4.1 Areas and Perimeters

Area measures the space inside a shape (like how much paint you need for a wall), while perimeter measures the distance around it (like how much fencing you need for a garden).

Rectangle: Area = length × width, Perimeter = 2(length + width)
Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height, Perimeter = sum of all sides
Circle: Area = πr², Circumference = 2πr
Parallelogram: Area = base × height (not the slant side!)
Trapezium: Area = ½ × (sum of parallel sides) × height
Example: Find the area and perimeter of a rectangle with length 8 cm and width 5 cm.
Area = 8 × 5 = 40 cm²
Perimeter = 2(8 + 5) = 2(13) = 26 cm
Real-world application: Buying carpet by area, calculating paint needed for walls, measuring athletic fields, designing landscaping, and calculating material costs for construction all use area and perimeter formulas.

4.2 Volumes and Surface Areas

Volume measures the space inside a 3D shape (like how much water a container can hold), while surface area measures the total area of all faces (like how much wrapping paper you need).

Cube: Volume = edge³, Surface Area = 6 × edge²
Cuboid: Volume = length × width × height, Surface Area = 2(lw + lh + wh)
Cylinder: Volume = πr²h, Total Surface Area = 2πr(r + h)
Sphere: Volume = (4/3)πr³, Surface Area = 4πr²
Cone: Volume = (1/3)πr²h, Total Surface Area = πr(r + l) where l = slant height
For any prism or cylinder, think of the volume as the area of the base multiplied by the height - like stacking identical slices on top of each other.
Example: Calculate the volume and surface area of a cube with edge length 4 cm.
Volume = 4³ = 64 cm³
Surface Area = 6 × 4² = 6 × 16 = 96 cm²
Real-world application: Package design uses surface area calculations. Storage containers are measured by volume. Construction projects calculate concrete volume. Water tanks, shipping containers, and food packaging all require volume and surface area measurements.

5. STATISTICS AND PROBABILITY

Statistics helps us make sense of data and draw conclusions from it. Probability helps us understand chance and uncertainty. Together, they form the basis of data science and decision-making in an uncertain world.

5.1 Data Representation

Data representation involves organizing information visually to make it easier to understand patterns, trends, and outliers - turning numbers into insights.

Types of data representation:
- Frequency tables: Organize raw data into categories with counts
- Bar charts/histograms: Compare quantities across categories
- Pie charts: Show proportions of a whole
- Line graphs: Display trends over time
- Scatter diagrams: Show relationships between two variables
When creating a pie chart, calculate the angle for each sector using:
Angle = (Item's frequency / Total frequency) × 360°
For example, if 25% of students prefer math, their sector would be 25% × 360° = 90°
Real-world application: Weather forecasts use line graphs to show temperature trends. Companies use pie charts to display market share. News media use bar charts to compare election results. Health dashboards use various charts to track disease spread.

5.2 Measures of Central Tendency

These are single values that aim to represent or summarize an entire dataset - like finding the "center" or "typical value" of the data.

Mean (arithmetic): Sum of all values divided by the number of values (the "average")
Median: Middle value when data is arranged in order (the "middle")
Mode: Most frequently occurring value (the "most common")
Example: Find the mean, median, and mode of: 4, 7, 9, 3, 5, 9, 8
Mean = (4+7+9+3+5+9+8)/7 = 45/7 = 6.43
Median (ordered data: 3,4,5,7,8,9,9): 7
Mode: 9 (occurs twice)
Different measures are useful in different situations:
- Mean uses all data points but is affected by outliers
- Median isn't affected by extreme values (good for income data)
- Mode shows the most common value (good for categorical data)
Real-world application: Average temperatures in weather reports (mean), median home prices in real estate (less affected by extremely expensive houses), and most popular shoe sizes in inventory management (mode).

5.3 Measures of Dispersion

These measure how spread out the data is - how much variation exists. Two datasets might have the same average but very different spreads.

Range = Maximum value - Minimum value (simplest measure of spread)
Variance = ∑(x - μ)² / n (average of squared differences from the mean)
Standard deviation = √Variance (most common measure of spread)
Example: Calculate the range of: 5, 8, 12, 15, 20
Range = 20 - 5 = 15
Real-world application: Quality control in manufacturing uses standard deviation to ensure products are consistent. Investment risk assessment uses variance to measure volatility. Educational testing uses standard deviation to compare student performance.

5.4 Probability

Probability measures how likely an event is to occur, from impossible (0) to certain (1). It's the mathematics of chance and uncertainty.

P(Event) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B) (addition rule)
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A) (multiplication rule)
For independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
Example: When rolling a fair six-sided die, find:
P(rolling a 5) = 1/6
P(rolling an even number) = 3/6 = 1/2
P(rolling a number greater than 4) = 2/6 = 1/3
Probability facts:
0 ≤ P(Event) ≤ 1 (probability is always between 0 and 1)
P(Certain event) = 1 (like getting either heads OR tails when flipping a coin)
P(Impossible event) = 0 (like rolling a 7 on a six-sided die)
P(Not A) = 1 - P(A) (complement rule)
Real-world application: Weather forecasts use probability (30% chance of rain). Insurance companies calculate risk using probability. Games of chance rely on probability principles. Medical diagnoses use probability to assess likelihood of conditions based on test results.

6. VECTORS AND MATRICES

Vectors and matrices are powerful mathematical tools that handle multiple values at once. They're essential in physics, computer graphics, data science, and many other fields.

6.1 Vectors

Unlike regular numbers (scalars) that only have magnitude, vectors have both magnitude AND direction. Think of them as arrows pointing in space.

Vector addition: v + w (connect the vectors head-to-tail)
Scalar multiplication: kv (stretches or shrinks the vector)
Magnitude of a vector |v| = √(v₁² + v₂² + v₃²) (length of the arrow)
Unit vector: v/|v| (same direction but length 1)
Dot product: v·w = |v||w|cos θ = v₁w₁ + v₂w₂ + v₃w₃ (product of magnitudes × cosine of angle between)
Example: Find the magnitude of v = 3i + 4j
|v| = √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5
[Imagine an arrow drawn from the origin to the point (3,4) on a coordinate plane. The length of this arrow is 5 units.]
Real-world application: Forces in physics are vectors (with magnitude and direction). Wind velocity is a vector. Navigation systems use vectors to calculate paths. Computer graphics use vectors to move objects on screen and create animations.

6.2 Matrices

Matrices are rectangular arrays of numbers arranged in rows and columns. Think of them as organized tables of values that can represent systems of equations, transformations, and data sets.

Matrix addition: (A + B)ᵢⱼ = Aᵢⱼ + Bᵢⱼ (add corresponding elements)
Scalar multiplication: (kA)ᵢⱼ = kAᵢⱼ (multiply each element by k)
Matrix multiplication: (AB)ᵢⱼ = ∑ Aᵢₖ × Bₖⱼ (row by column multiplication)
Determinant of 2×2 matrix: |A| = ad - bc, where A = [[a, b], [c, d]]
Inverse of 2×2 matrix: A⁻¹ = 1/|A| × [[d, -b], [-c, a]], where A = [[a, b], [c, d]]
Example: Find the determinant of A = [[3, 2], [1, 4]]
|A| = 3×4 - 2×1 = 12 - 2 = 10
Matrix multiplication is not commutative in general: AB ≠ BA (unlike regular multiplication)
A matrix must be square to have a determinant
A matrix has an inverse if and only if its determinant is not zero (just like how a number can't be divided by zero)
Real-world application: Computer graphics use matrices for transformations like rotation and scaling. Search engines like Google use matrices in their ranking algorithms. Economics uses matrices for input-output models. Quantum mechanics represents states as matrices.

7. CALCULUS

Calculus is the mathematics of change and accumulation. Developed independently by Newton and Leibniz, it's one of the most powerful tools in mathematics, allowing us to analyze things that are continuously changing.

7.1 Differentiation

Differentiation finds the rate at which something is changing - like the speed of a car (rate of change of position) or the slope of a curve at any point. It's about finding instantaneous rates of change.

Basic rules:
- d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹ (power rule)
- d/dx(k) = 0 (k is constant - constants don't change)
- d/dx(kf(x)) = k·d/dx(f(x)) (constant multiple rule)
- d/dx(f(x) + g(x)) = d/dx(f(x)) + d/dx(g(x)) (sum rule)
- d/dx(f(x) × g(x)) = f(x)·d/dx(g(x)) + g(x)·d/dx(f(x)) (product rule)
Example: Find the derivative of f(x) = 3x² - 2x + 5
f'(x) = 3·2x¹ - 2·1x⁰ + 0 = 6x - 2
The derivative at a point gives you the slope of the tangent line at that point. If f'(a) = 0, then the function has a horizontal tangent at x = a, which often indicates a maximum or minimum.
Real-world application: Economists use derivatives to find marginal cost and revenue. Engineers use them to optimize designs. Physicists use them to describe motion. Medical researchers use them to model the spread of diseases.

7.2 Integration

Integration is the reverse of differentiation - it finds the total accumulation over an interval. Think of it as adding up infinitely many infinitesimally small pieces, like finding the total distance traveled based on speed over time.

Basic rules:
- ∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) + C (n ≠ -1) (power rule for integration)
- ∫k dx = kx + C (k is constant)
- ∫(f(x) + g(x)) dx = ∫f(x) dx + ∫g(x) dx (sum rule)
- ∫k·f(x) dx = k·∫f(x) dx (constant multiple rule)
The "+ C" is the constant of integration and represents the fact that many functions share the same derivative. For example, x², x²+1, and x²-5 all have a derivative of 2x.

Definite integral: ∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a), where F is the antiderivative of f. This calculates the net accumulated value from x=a to x=b.
Example: Evaluate ∫02 (3x² + 2) dx
∫(3x² + 2) dx = 3x³/3 + 2x + C = x³ + 2x + C
∫02 (3x² + 2) dx = (2³ + 2·2) - (0³ + 2·0) = (8 + 4) - 0 = 12
Real-world application: Physics uses integration to find work done by a force. Economics uses it to calculate total profit from marginal profit. Probability uses it to find the probability over a range of a continuous random variable. Engineering uses it to calculate centers of mass and moments of inertia.
Geometrically, the definite integral represents the area under the curve between the specified limits. This visual interpretation can help understand what integration is actually calculating.

8. EXAMINATION TIPS

8.1 General Tips

  • Read each question carefully, identifying what is given and what is required.
  • Show all your working clearly and systematically.
  • Manage your time wisely, spending more time on higher-mark questions.
  • Check your answers if time permits.
  • Ensure your calculator is set to the correct mode (degrees/radians) for trigonometric calculations.

8.2 Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to include units in your final answer.
  • Computational errors in basic arithmetic.
  • Not reading the question carefully and missing key information.
  • Forgetting to check if your answer makes sense in the context of the problem.
  • Incorrect application of formulas.

8.3 Last-Minute Preparation

  • Review key formulas, properties, and theorems.
  • Practice with past WAEC questions to get familiar with the format and style.
  • Focus on areas where you have challenges.
  • Ensure you have all required materials (calculator, mathematical set, etc.) ready before the exam day.
  • Get adequate rest before the examination.
