How I Start My Trading Day

Defining the "Real Trading Window"

Not all trading hours are created equal. The real trading window refers to the specific times when market conditions align to provide optimal trading opportunities. During these windows, we see:

  • Highest liquidity levels
  • Tightest spreads
  • Clear directional moves
  • Institutional participation

Trading outside these windows often results in choppy, unpredictable price action that leads to unnecessary losses. By limiting our trading to these premium hours, we dramatically increase our win rate.

The forex and gold markets operate 24 hours a day, but activity varies significantly across different sessions. Here's how to identify the best times:

Why does the overlap matter so much? When two major financial centers are trading simultaneously:

  • Spreads tighten to their lowest levels
  • Institutional orders create clear directional momentum
  • Liquidity pools are more easily identified and swept

Time Frame

Time frame selection is crucial for precision trading. The wrong time frame can lead to premature entries, noise-filled signals, or missed opportunities. This chapter reveals the exact time frame combination that maximizes setup quality while maintaining practical execution.

Main Chart: 15-Minute (M15)

The 15-minute chart serves as my primary execution time frame. This is where I identify entry triggers and place trades. Here's why M15 is optimal:

  • Precision Timing: Provides clear entry points without excessive noise
  • Swing Trade Duration: Trades typically last 2-8 hours, fitting within a single trading session
  • Clean Structure: Market structure shifts are clearly visible
  • Practical Execution: Enough time to analyze and execute without rushed decisions
  • Realistic Targets: 1:7 risk-reward ratios are achievable within reasonable timeframes

On the M15 chart, I look for specific entry patterns that will be detailed in Chapter 4. The key is that M15 provides granular detail without getting lost in the noise of lower time frames like M5 or M1.

Higher Time Frame Bias: H1 & H4

While M15 is our execution chart, we never trade in isolation. Higher time frames provide the essential context and directional bias:

4-Hour Chart (H4) ‚Äî The Strategic Overview

The H4 chart reveals the overall market structure and long-term trend:

  • Identifies major market structure (bullish, bearish, or ranging)
  • Shows weekly and daily key levels
  • Reveals large imbalances and liquidity pools
  • Determines whether we should be looking for longs or shorts

1-Hour Chart (H1) ‚Äî The Tactical Bridge

The H1 chart bridges the gap between strategic overview and tactical execution:

  • Confirms the H4 bias with more recent price action
  • Identifies interim structure breaks
  • Shows session-specific liquidity levels
  • Provides recent swing points for stop-loss placement

üìä Time Frame Hierarchy

H4: "Where are we going?" (Overall direction)
H1: "How are we getting there?" (Recent structure)
M15: "When do I enter?" (Precise timing)

Combining HTF Bias with M15 Entries

The magic happens when we align all three time frames. Here's the step-by-step process:

Step 1: Establish H4 Bias

Every trading session begins with H4 analysis:

  • Is price making higher highs and higher lows? (Bullish bias)
  • Is price making lower highs and lower lows? (Bearish bias)
  • Is price trapped in a range? (No clear bias‚Äîwait for breakout)

Step 2: Confirm with H1 Structure

Once H4 bias is established, zoom into H1:

  • Does H1 structure support the H4 bias?
  • Has there been a recent market structure shift (MSS) in the direction of H4 bias?
  • Where are the most recent swing points for stop-loss placement?

Step 3: Execute on M15 Confirmation

With HTF bias confirmed, wait for M15 entry trigger:

  • Liquidity sweep of recent high/low
  • Market structure shift on M15
  • Displacement candle showing institutional entry
  • Entry into imbalance or order block

üìà Multi-Time Frame Example

Scenario: EUR/USD Long Setup

H4 Analysis: Price is making higher highs and higher lows. Clear uptrend. Recent H4 candle closed above previous swing high. Bias = BULLISH.

H1 Analysis: Price swept the Asian session low, then broke above the Asian high creating a market structure shift. Recent swing low at 1.0850. Bias confirmed = LONG.

M15 Execution: Waited for price to sweep liquidity below the recent M15 low at 1.0865, then observed strong displacement candle back up breaking M15 structure. Entry at 1.0870 with stop below the sweep at 1.0860 (10 pips risk). Target at 1.0940 (70 pips reward = 1:7 RR).

Result: All three time frames aligned. Trade executed with confidence. Price hit target within 6 hours.

The Multi-Time Frame Advantage

Trading with multi-time frame analysis provides several critical advantages:

  • Higher Probability: Alignment across time frames increases win rate
  • Reduced False Signals: HTF context filters out counter-trend noise
  • Better Risk Management: Clear invalidation points from HTF structure
  • Confidence: Knowing the "big picture" supports conviction in trades
  • Patience: Understanding HTF bias helps you wait for optimal entries

‚ö†Ô∏è Never Trade Against HTF Bias

This is a cardinal rule: if H4 is bullish and H1 confirms, do NOT take short entries on M15 even if you see a perfect bearish setup. Counter-trend trades might work occasionally, but over time they destroy accounts. Always trade WITH the higher time frame bias.

CHAPTER 04

Entries & Exits

This is where theory meets practice. Entry and exit rules are the heart of any trading strategy. This chapter provides the exact criteria for entering trades, placing stop losses, and taking profits with surgical precision.

Entry Rules: The Three-Step Confirmation

Every trade must satisfy three mandatory conditions before execution. Missing even one condition means NO TRADE.

Condition 1: Liquidity Sweep

Price must first sweep a liquidity pool. Liquidity pools are areas where stop losses cluster, typically:

  • Above/below recent swing highs/lows
  • Above/below round numbers (e.g., 1.1000, 1950.00 for gold)
  • Asian session range extremes
  • Previous day high/low

A liquidity sweep occurs when price briefly moves beyond these levels to trigger stops, then quickly reverses. This "stop hunt" is institutional behavior‚Äîthey need liquidity to fill large orders.

Condition 2: Market Structure Shift (MSS)

After the liquidity sweep, price must break structure in the opposite direction:

  • For longs: Price must break above the most recent lower high
  • For shorts: Price must break below the most recent higher low

The market structure shift signals that institutional orders have been filled and the true direction is now unfolding.

Condition 3: Confirmation & Entry Trigger

After the MSS, wait for price to retrace into a premium area:

  • Order Block: The last opposite-colored candle before the strong move
  • Fair Value Gap (Imbalance): The gap left by the displacement candle
  • Fibonacci Level: 0.618 or 0.786 retracement of the impulse move

Entry is taken when price enters this zone AND shows a strong displacement candle (large candle with minimal wick) in the direction of bias.

‚úÖ Entry Checklist

  • ‚òë HTF bias confirmed (H4 and H1 aligned)
  • ‚òë Liquidity sweep observed (stop hunt above/below key level)
  • ‚òë Market structure shift on M15 (break of structure in bias direction)
  • ‚òë Price retraced into premium zone (order block, FVG, or Fib level)
  • ‚òë Strong displacement candle confirming entry
  • ‚òë Trading during optimal liquidity hours (London/NY overlap)
  • ‚òë No high-impact news scheduled within next 2 hours

If ALL boxes are checked ‚Üí Execute trade. If any box is unchecked ‚Üí Wait for next setup.

Stop Loss Placement: Protecting Capital

Stop loss placement is non-negotiable. Every single trade must have a stop loss placed immediately upon entry.

Stop Loss Rules

Place your stop loss beyond the invalidation point:

  • For longs: 5-10 pips below the liquidity sweep low
  • For shorts: 5-10 pips above the liquidity sweep high

The extra 5-10 pips (or equivalent for gold) accounts for spread and minor volatility. Your stop should not be so tight that normal price fluctuation triggers it, but not so wide that risk becomes unacceptable.

Stop Loss Example

GBP/USD long entry:

  • Liquidity sweep low: 1.2650
  • Entry price: 1.2670
  • Stop loss: 1.2640 (10 pips below sweep low)
  • Risk: 30 pips

‚ö†Ô∏è Never Move Stop Loss Against Your Position

Once a stop loss is placed, you may move it to breakeven or trailing stop in profit, but NEVER move it further away from entry to "give the trade more room." This is how accounts blow up. If price hits your stop, the setup was invalid‚Äîaccept it and move on.

Take Profit: Targeting Liquidity

Take profit targets are based on the next significant liquidity pool or imbalance in the direction of your trade.

Take Profit Targets (Priority Order)

  1. Next Major Liquidity Pool: Swing high/low, round number, or session extreme
  2. Unfilled Imbalance: Previous fair value gap that hasn't been filled
  3. Key Technical Level: Daily/weekly high or low, major support/resistance

Measure the distance to your target. For a 1:7 risk-reward ratio, your target must be at least 7 times your stop loss distance.

Take Profit Example

Continuing the GBP/USD example:

  • Entry: 1.2670
  • Stop: 1.2640 (30 pips risk)
  • Target: Previous day high at 1.2880 (210 pips reward)
  • Risk-Reward: 1:7

‚úÖ Exit Checklist

  • ‚òë Stop loss placed immediately upon entry
  • ‚òë Stop loss positioned beyond liquidity sweep point
  • ‚òë Take profit target identified at next liquidity pool
  • ‚òë Risk-reward ratio minimum 1:7 verified
  • ‚òë Take profit order placed (or alert set)
  • ‚òë Position size calculated based on stop loss distance
  • ‚òë Trade logged in journal with screenshots

Risk & Reward

Risk management separates professional traders from gamblers. You can have the best entry strategy in the world, but without proper risk control, long-term success is impossible. This chapter covers the exact risk parameters that have kept my account growing consistently.

The 1:7 Risk-Reward Target

The foundation of this strategy is the minimum 1:7 risk-reward ratio. This means for every dollar you risk, you aim to make at least seven dollars.

Why 1:7 Matters

With a 1:7 RR ratio, you only need to win 30% of your trades to be profitable:

Trades Winners (30%) Losers (70%) Net Result
10 trades 3 wins √ó $700 = $2,100 7 losses √ó -$100 = -$700 +$1,400 profit
20 trades 6 wins √ó $700 = $4,200 14 losses √ó -$100 = -$1,400 +$2,800 profit

Even with a 30% win rate, you're profitable. With proper execution, a 40-50% win rate is achievable, which produces exceptional returns.

üí° The Math of Success

At 40% win rate with 1:7 RR over 100 trades:

  • 40 winners √ó $700 = $28,000
  • 60 losers √ó -$100 = -$6,000
  • Net profit: $22,000

This is why patient, quality setups with excellent RR ratios outperform high-frequency trading with mediocre ratios.

Risk Per Trade: The 1-2% Rule

Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account on any single trade. This is non-negotiable.

Why This Matters

Conservative risk management ensures you can survive losing streaks:

  • At 1% risk: You can lose 10 trades in a row and still have 90% of your account
  • At 2% risk: You can lose 10 trades in a row and still have 82% of your account
  • At 10% risk: You can lose 10 trades in a row and your account is virtually destroyed

Losing streaks happen to everyone. Proper risk management ensures they're temporary setbacks, not career-ending disasters.

Position Sizing: The Formula

Position sizing determines how many lots (or contracts) to trade based on your risk parameters.

Position Size = (Account Size √ó Risk %) √∑ Stop Loss Distance

Position Sizing Examples

Example 1: EUR/USD

  • Account size: $10,000
  • Risk per trade: 1% = $100
  • Stop loss distance: 20 pips
  • Position size: $100 √∑ (20 pips √ó $10 per pip) = 0.5 standard lots

Example 2: XAU/USD (Gold)

  • Account size: $5,000
  • Risk per trade: 2% = $100
  • Stop loss distance: $5.00
  • Position size: $100 √∑ $5.00 = 20 micro lots (0.20 lots)

üìè Position Sizing Steps

  1. Determine your account size
  2. Calculate risk amount (1-2% of account)
  3. Measure stop loss distance in pips or dollars
  4. Divide risk amount by stop loss distance
  5. Round down to the nearest tradeable lot size

Always round down, never up. It's better to risk slightly less than planned than to exceed your risk limit.

Practical Risk Management Rules

Daily Loss Limit

Set a maximum daily loss limit of 3-4% of your account. If you hit this limit, stop trading for the day. Take a break, review what went wrong, and come back fresh the next day.

Maximum Concurrent Trades

Limit yourself to 2-3 open positions maximum. More trades mean more risk and divided attention. Quality over quantity applies to position management too.

Never Average Down

If a trade goes against you, do NOT add to the position to "average your entry price." This is a recipe for disaster. Accept the loss if your stop is hit and wait for the next setup.

Weekly Risk Limit

Consider implementing a weekly loss limit of 6-8% of your account. If you hit this limit, take a break from trading for the remainder of the week. Use this time to review your trades, refine your analysis, and reset mentally.

Psychology & Trade Selection

Risk management isn't just about numbers‚Äîit's about mindset. The psychological aspect of trading is what separates consistent winners from those who blow up accounts.

Quality Over Quantity

This strategy is designed for precision, not frequency. Some days you might take zero trades. That's perfectly fine. It's better to wait for one perfect 1:7 setup than to force five mediocre 1:2 setups.

Top traders average 1-3 trades per day. They're not glued to their screens, constantly entering and exiting. They wait patiently for their edge to appear, then execute with confidence.

Emotional Control Principles

  • Detachment from Outcomes: Focus on executing the process correctly, not on individual trade outcomes
  • Accept Losses: Losses are part of the business. A loss doesn't mean you failed‚Äîit means you managed risk properly
  • No Revenge Trading: After a loss, resist the urge to immediately "win it back." This leads to poor decision-making
  • Celebrate Process, Not Profits: Take pride in following your rules, regardless of the result
  • Stay Humble: Winning streaks end. Don't increase risk during hot streaks‚Äîvariance catches everyone

‚ö†Ô∏è The Revenge Trading Trap

Revenge trading is the fastest way to destroy an account. After taking a loss, your brain wants to "make it back" immediately. This emotional state causes you to:

  • Take suboptimal setups that don't meet all criteria
  • Increase position size beyond your risk rules
  • Force trades when no valid setup exists

If you feel emotional after a loss, step away from the charts. Go for a walk. Review the trade objectively. Come back when you're calm and ready to follow the process.

Trade Selection Criteria

Not every setup that appears valid should be taken. Here are additional filters for selecting only the highest-quality trades:

The "A+ Setup" Filter

Ask yourself these questions before entering:

  1. Is HTF bias crystal clear? If you have any doubt about H4/H1 direction, skip the trade
  2. Are all three entry conditions met? Liquidity sweep + MSS + confirmation‚Äîall must be present
  3. Is the RR ratio at least 1:7? If not, the trade doesn't meet strategy criteria
  4. Is this during optimal hours? London/NY overlap gets priority
  5. Do I feel calm and confident? If you're anxious or uncertain, skip it

If the answer to any question is "no," don't take the trade. Wait for the next opportunity.

  • Overtrading: Taking suboptimal setups out of boredom or desire for action
  • Ignoring HTF Bias: Trading against higher time frame direction
  • Moving Stop Losses: Giving losing trades "more room" instead of accepting the loss
  • Increasing Risk After Wins: Getting overconfident and risking more than planned
  • Revenge Trading: Trying to immediately recover losses with emotional trades
  • Comparing to Others: Your trading journey is unique‚Äîfocus on your own progress

Measuring Progress

Success in trading isn't measured by daily profits, but by consistent execution over time. Track these metrics:

  • Rule Adherence Rate: What percentage of trades followed all rules? (Target: 100%)
  • Win Rate: Percentage of winning trades (Target: 35-50%)
  • Average RR: Average risk-reward of your trades (Target: 1:7+)
  • Expectancy: Average profit per trade over many trades (Target: positive)
  • Maximum Drawdown: Largest peak-to-trough decline (Target: <15%)
