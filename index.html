Chapter One

Introduction to Islam

Islam is a monotheistic religion that emerged in the 7th century CE in the Arabian Peninsula. The word "Islam" comes from the Arabic root s-l-m, which means peace, safety, and submission to Allah (God). A person who practices Islam is called a Muslim, meaning "one who submits to Allah."

وَمَا أَرْسَلْنَاكَ إِلَّا رَحْمَةً لِّلْعَالَمِينَ

And We have not sent you except as a mercy to all the worlds.

— Quran 21:107

Islam is founded on five fundamental principles known as the Five Pillars, and Muslims believe in six articles of faith. The religion emphasizes the oneness of Allah, the prophethood of Muhammad (peace be upon him), and the importance of living a life in accordance with divine guidance.

Key Concepts in Islam:

  • Tawhid (Unity of Allah) - The fundamental belief in the oneness of God
  • Risalah (Prophethood) - The belief in divine messengers
  • Akhirah (Afterlife) - The belief in life after death and judgment
  • Submission and Peace - Living in harmony with Allah's will
❋

The Meaning of Submission

In Islamic theology, submission to Allah does not mean passive resignation but rather an active choice to align one's life with divine guidance. This submission brings inner peace and spiritual fulfillment, as believers trust in Allah's wisdom and mercy.

"None of you truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Chapter Two

The Five Pillars of Islam

The Five Pillars of Islam represent the foundation of Muslim life. These are the essential acts of worship and practice that define what it means to be a Muslim. Each pillar serves both as a spiritual obligation and a means of connecting with Allah and the Muslim community.

1. Shahada (Declaration of Faith)

لَا إِلَٰهَ إِلَّا ٱللَّٰهُ مُحَمَّدٌ رَسُولُ ٱللَّٰهِ

The Shahada is the Islamic declaration of faith: "There is no god but Allah, and Muhammad is the messenger of Allah." This testimony is the foundation of Islamic belief and the gateway to becoming a Muslim.

2. Salah (Prayer)

Muslims are required to perform five daily prayers at prescribed times: Fajr (dawn), Dhuhr (midday), Asr (afternoon), Maghrib (sunset), and Isha (night). These prayers serve as a direct connection between the worshipper and Allah.

Benefits of Regular Prayer:

  • Spiritual purification and mindfulness
  • Regular remembrance of Allah throughout the day
  • Discipline and time management
  • Community bonding when performed in congregation

3. Zakat (Charitable Giving)

Zakat is the obligatory charity that Muslims must give annually, typically 2.5% of their accumulated wealth. This pillar promotes social justice and helps support the less fortunate in society.

4. Sawm (Fasting during Ramadan)

During the holy month of Ramadan, Muslims fast from dawn to sunset, abstaining from food, drink, and other physical needs. This practice develops self-discipline, empathy for the less fortunate, and spiritual awareness.

5. Hajj (Pilgrimage to Mecca)

Muslims who are physically and financially able must perform the Hajj pilgrimage to Mecca at least once in their lifetime. This journey represents spiritual purification and unity with Muslims from around the world.

And it is Allah's right upon the people that they perform Hajj of the House - whoever is able to find a way.

— Quran 3:97

Chapter Three

The Six Articles of Faith

The Six Articles of Faith (Arkan al-Iman) represent the core beliefs that every Muslim must hold. These beliefs form the theological foundation of Islam and guide the believer's understanding of existence, purpose, and destiny.

1. Belief in Allah (Tawhid)

Muslims believe in the absolute oneness and uniqueness of Allah. This concept, known as Tawhid, is the most fundamental aspect of Islamic theology. Allah is eternal, all-knowing, all-powerful, and the creator and sustainer of all existence.

قُلْ هُوَ اللَّهُ أَحَدٌ ۝ اللَّهُ الصَّمَدُ ۝ لَمْ يَلِدْ وَلَمْ يُولَدْ ۝ وَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُ كُفُوًا أَحَدٌ

Say: He is Allah, the One! Allah, the Eternal, Absolute. He begets not, nor is He begotten. And there is none like unto Him.

— Quran 112:1-4 (Surah Al-Ikhlas)

2. Belief in Angels (Mala'ika)

Muslims believe in the existence of angels as beings created by Allah from light. Angels are sinless, obedient servants of Allah who carry out His commands. Notable angels include Gabriel (Jibril), who delivered revelations to the prophets.

3. Belief in Divine Books

Muslims believe that Allah revealed divine books to guide humanity throughout history. These include the Torah (Tawrat) given to Moses, the Psalms (Zabur) given to David, the Gospel (Injil) given to Jesus, and finally the Quran given to Muhammad (peace be upon him).

The Divine Books:

  • Tawrat (Torah) - Revealed to Prophet Musa (Moses)
  • Zabur (Psalms) - Revealed to Prophet Dawud (David)
  • Injil (Gospel) - Revealed to Prophet Isa (Jesus)
  • Quran - Revealed to Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him)

4. Belief in Prophets and Messengers

Muslims believe that Allah sent prophets and messengers throughout history to guide different communities. Islam recognizes 25 prophets mentioned by name in the Quran, including Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad (peace be upon them all).

5. Belief in the Day of Judgment

Muslims believe in life after death and the Day of Judgment, when all humans will be held accountable for their actions in this world. This belief encourages moral behavior and gives meaning to earthly existence.

6. Belief in Divine Decree (Qadar)

Muslims believe in Allah's divine decree - that Allah has knowledge of all things and that nothing occurs without His will. However, this does not negate human free will and responsibility for one's actions.

"Faith is to believe in Allah, His angels, His books, His messengers, the Last Day, and to believe in divine decree, both good and bad."

— Sahih Muslim

Chapter Four

The Quran: The Final Revelation

The Quran is the holy book of Islam, believed by Muslims to be the direct word of Allah as revealed to Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) through the angel Gabriel (Jibril). The name "Quran" comes from the Arabic root q-r-a, meaning "to read" or "to recite."

إِنَّا نَحْنُ نَزَّلْنَا الذِّكْرَ وَإِنَّا لَهُ لَحَافِظُونَ

Indeed it is We who sent down the Quran and indeed, We will be its guardian.

— Quran 15:9

Structure and Composition

The Quran consists of 114 chapters called "Surahs," which vary in length from 3 to 286 verses called "Ayahs." The chapters are arranged roughly in order of decreasing length, with the exception of the opening chapter, Al-Fatiha.

Key Features of the Quran:

  • 114 Surahs (chapters) with 6,236 Ayahs (verses)
  • Revealed over a period of approximately 23 years
  • Written in classical Arabic with unmatched literary beauty
  • Memorized by millions of Muslims worldwide
  • Preserved in its original language without alteration

Themes and Content

The Quran addresses various themes including theology, guidance for personal conduct, legislation, and stories of previous prophets. It emphasizes the oneness of Allah, moral righteousness, social justice, and preparation for the afterlife.

الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ رَبِّ الْعَالَمِينَ ۝ الرَّحْمَٰنِ الرَّحِيمِ ۝ مَالِكِ يَوْمِ الدِّينِ

The opening verses of Al-Fatiha translate to: "All praise is due to Allah, Lord of all the worlds, The Most Gracious, the Most Merciful, Master of the Day of Judgment."

The Science of Quranic Recitation (Tajweed)

Tajweed is the art of Quranic recitation that ensures the proper pronunciation and intonation of each letter and word. This science preserves the original way the Quran was revealed and recited by Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).

"The one who is skilled in the recitation of the Quran will be with the honorable and obedient scribes (angels), and the one who recites the Quran and finds it difficult will have two rewards."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Chapter Five

Prophet Muhammad (Peace be upon him)

Muhammad ibn Abdullah (peace be upon him) was born in Mecca around 570 CE. Muslims believe he is the final messenger of Allah, sent to guide humanity with the message of Islam. His life serves as a perfect example for Muslims to follow.

Early Life

Muhammad (peace be upon him) was born into the Quraysh tribe. He lost his father before birth and his mother when he was six years old. Raised by his grandfather and later his uncle, he became known for his honesty and trustworthiness, earning the nickname "Al-Amin" (the trustworthy).

Key Events in the Prophet's Life:

  • 570 CE - Born in Mecca during the Year of the Elephant
  • 610 CE - Received first revelation in Cave Hira
  • 622 CE - Migration (Hijra) to Medina
  • 630 CE - Peaceful conquest of Mecca
  • 632 CE - Farewell pilgrimage and passing away

The First Revelation

At age 40, while meditating in the Cave of Hira, Muhammad (peace be upon him) received his first revelation from Allah through the angel Gabriel. This marked the beginning of his prophetic mission.

اقْرَأْ بِاسْمِ رَبِّكَ الَّذِي خَلَقَ ۝ خَلَقَ الْإِنسَانَ مِنْ عَلَقٍ ۝ اقْرَأْ وَرَبُّكَ الْأَكْرَمُ

Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clot. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous.

— Quran 96:1-3

The Migration to Medina (Hijra)

Facing persecution in Mecca, the Prophet and his followers migrated to Medina in 622 CE. This event, known as the Hijra, marks the beginning of the Islamic calendar and was crucial for the establishment of the first Muslim community.

Character and Teachings

The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) exemplified the highest moral character. He was known for his kindness, justice, humility, and forgiveness. His teachings emphasized the importance of treating all people with respect and dignity.

"I have been sent to perfect good character."

— Al-Adab al-Mufrad

The Final Sermon

During his farewell pilgrimage, the Prophet delivered a final sermon that summarized key Islamic principles including equality, justice, and the sanctity of life and property. This sermon remains a foundational text for understanding Islamic values.

Chapter Six

The Sunnah and Hadith

The Sunnah refers to the way of life of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), including his actions, sayings, and approvals. Hadith are the recorded sayings, actions, and approvals of the Prophet that serve as the second source of Islamic law and guidance after the Quran.

The Importance of Sunnah

The Sunnah provides practical guidance on how to implement Quranic teachings in daily life. It explains, clarifies, and provides context for the Quranic revelations, making it an essential source for understanding Islam.

وَمَا آتَاكُمُ الرَّسُولُ فَخُذُوهُ وَمَا نَهَاكُمْ عَنْهُ فَانتَهُوا

And whatever the Messenger has given you - take it; and what he has forbidden you - refrain from it.

— Quran 59:7

Classification of Hadith

Islamic scholars have developed a sophisticated system for classifying hadith based on their authenticity and chain of transmission (isnad). The main categories are:

Types of Hadith:

  • Sahih (Authentic) - Highest level of authenticity
  • Hasan (Good) - Acceptable but slightly lower than Sahih
  • Da'if (Weak) - Contains flaws in transmission
  • Mawdu (Fabricated) - False attributions to the Prophet

Major Hadith Collections

Several renowned scholars compiled authentic hadith collections. The most famous are the "Sahih Sitta" (Six Authentic Collections), with Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim being considered the most authentic.

❋

The Science of Hadith

Islamic scholars developed rigorous methods to verify the authenticity of hadith, examining both the chain of narrators and the content. This scientific approach ensured that only reliable traditions were accepted as guidance.

"The example of guidance and knowledge with which Allah has sent me is like abundant rain falling on the earth, some of which was fertile soil that absorbed rain water and brought forth vegetation and grass in abundance."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Chapter Seven

Islamic Law (Shariah)

Shariah, often translated as "Islamic Law," literally means "the path to water" or "the clear path." It represents the comprehensive framework of divine guidance that governs all aspects of Muslim life, derived from the Quran and Sunnah.

Sources of Islamic Law

Islamic jurisprudence (Fiqh) derives its principles from four main sources, known as the "Usul al-Fiqh" (Principles of Islamic Jurisprudence):

The Four Sources of Islamic Law:

  • Quran - The primary source of divine guidance
  • Sunnah - The teachings and practices of Prophet Muhammad
  • Ijma - Scholarly consensus on religious matters
  • Qiyas - Analogical reasoning based on established principles

Categories of Islamic Law

Islamic law traditionally covers five categories of human actions, known as the "Ahkam" (rulings):

Fard/Wajib (Obligatory): Actions that are mandatory and rewarded when performed, punished when neglected.

Mustahabb/Sunnah (Recommended): Actions that are encouraged and rewarded when performed, but not punished when omitted.

Mubah (Permissible): Actions that are neutral - neither rewarded nor punished.

Makruh (Discouraged): Actions that are disliked but not forbidden.

Haram (Forbidden): Actions that are prohibited and punished when committed.

Schools of Islamic Jurisprudence

Over time, different schools of Islamic jurisprudence (madhabs) developed, each with slightly different methodologies while maintaining the same fundamental sources:

Major Sunni Schools:

  • Hanafi - Emphasizes reasoning and local customs
  • Maliki - Focuses on practices of Medina
  • Shafi'i - Systematic approach to legal methodology
  • Hanbali - Conservative approach emphasizing text

وَأَنِ احْكُم بَيْنَهُم بِمَا أَنزَلَ اللَّهُ وَلَا تَتَّبِعْ أَهْوَاءَهُمْ

And judge between them by what Allah has revealed and do not follow their inclinations.

— Quran 5:49

Contemporary Application

Modern Muslim scholars work to apply Islamic legal principles to contemporary issues, balancing traditional interpretations with modern realities while maintaining the core spiritual and ethical values of Islam.

Chapter Eight

Islamic Ethics and Morality

Islamic ethics, known as Akhlaq, encompasses the moral principles and values that guide Muslim behavior. These ethics are derived from the Quran and the Sunnah (teachings and practices) of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).

Core Moral Principles

Fundamental Virtues in Islam:

  • Justice (Adl) - Being fair and equitable in all dealings
  • Compassion (Rahma) - Showing mercy and kindness to all creation
  • Honesty (Sidq) - Truthfulness in speech and action
  • Patience (Sabr) - Perseverance through difficulties
  • Gratitude (Shukr) - Appreciation for Allah's blessings
  • Humility (Tawadu) - Modesty and absence of arrogance
  • Generosity (Karam) - Giving freely to others

Social Responsibility

Islam emphasizes the importance of community and social responsibility. Muslims are encouraged to contribute positively to society, help those in need, and work for justice and peace.

وَمَن أَحْيَاهَا فَكَأَنَّمَا أَحْيَا النَّاسَ جَمِيعًا

And whoever saves a life, it is as if he has saved all of mankind.

— Quran 5:32

Environmental Stewardship

Islam teaches that humans are trustees (khalifa) of the Earth and must care for the environment responsibly. This includes avoiding waste, protecting natural resources, and treating animals with kindness.

❋

The Golden Rule in Islam

The Islamic version of the Golden Rule emphasizes treating others with the same kindness and respect that one would wish to receive. This principle extends to all human relationships and interactions.

"The believers in their mutual kindness, compassion, and sympathy are just like one body; when one limb is affected, the whole body responds to it with wakefulness and fever."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Business Ethics

Islam provides detailed guidance on ethical business practices, emphasizing honesty, fairness, and the prohibition of usury (riba) and excessive uncertainty (gharar) in transactions.

Chapter Nine

Islamic Worship and Spirituality

Islamic worship extends beyond the Five Pillars to include various forms of devotion and spiritual practices that bring Muslims closer to Allah and purify their hearts and souls.

Dhikr (Remembrance of Allah)

Dhikr involves the continuous remembrance and mention of Allah through various phrases, prayers, and recitations. It can be performed silently or aloud, individually or in groups.

سُبْحَانَ اللهِ وَالْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ وَلَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللهُ وَاللهُ أَكْبَرُ

"Glory be to Allah, and praise be to Allah, and there is no deity but Allah, and Allah is Greatest."

Common Forms of Dhikr:

  • Tasbih - Glorification of Allah (SubhanAllah)
  • Tahmid - Praise of Allah (Alhamdulillah)
  • Tahlil - Declaration of Allah's oneness (La ilaha illa Allah)
  • Takbir - Proclamation of Allah's greatness (Allahu Akbar)
  • Istighfar - Seeking Allah's forgiveness

Du'a (Supplication)

Du'a is the act of calling upon Allah in prayer and supplication. Muslims are encouraged to make du'a at all times, asking for guidance, forgiveness, blessings, and help in both worldly and spiritual matters.

وَقَالَ رَبُّكُمُ ادْعُونِي أَسْتَجِبْ لَكُمْ

And your Lord says: Call upon Me; I will respond to you.

— Quran 40:60

Night Prayer (Tahajjud)

Tahajjud is the voluntary night prayer performed after sleeping and before dawn. It is considered one of the most spiritually rewarding acts of worship, offering a special time for intimate communion with Allah.

Fasting Beyond Ramadan

While fasting during Ramadan is obligatory, Muslims also engage in voluntary fasting throughout the year, such as on Mondays and Thursdays, or during the first ten days of Dhul Hijjah.

"Whoever fasts one day for the sake of Allah, Allah will distance his face from the Hellfire by seventy years."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Spiritual Purification (Tazkiyah)

Tazkiyah refers to the process of spiritual purification and self-development. It involves both external acts of worship and internal spiritual work to purify the heart from negative qualities and develop virtuous character.

Chapter Ten

Islamic History and Civilization

Islamic history spans over fourteen centuries, during which Muslim civilizations made significant contributions to human knowledge, culture, and society. Understanding this rich history provides insight into Islam's global impact.

The Rightly-Guided Caliphs (632-661 CE)

After Prophet Muhammad's death, the Muslim community was led by four successive caliphs known as the "Rightly-Guided Caliphs": Abu Bakr, Umar ibn al-Khattab, Uthman ibn Affan, and Ali ibn Abi Talib.

Achievements of the Early Caliphate:

  • Compilation and preservation of the Quran
  • Rapid expansion of Islamic territories
  • Establishment of administrative systems
  • Development of Islamic jurisprudence
  • Integration of diverse populations under Islamic rule

The Golden Age of Islam (8th-13th centuries)

During the Abbasid Caliphate and other Islamic dynasties, Muslim civilization experienced a golden age of learning, scientific advancement, and cultural flourishing.

❋

Major Islamic Empires

The Umayyad Caliphate (661-750 CE): Extended Islamic rule from Spain to Central Asia, establishing Arabic as the administrative language.

The Abbasid Caliphate (750-1258 CE): Centered in Baghdad, this period saw tremendous intellectual and cultural achievements.

The Ottoman Empire (1299-1922 CE): One of the longest-lasting empires in history, spanning three continents and lasting over 600 years.

وَجَعَلْنَا مِنْهُمْ أَئِمَّةً يَهْدُونَ بِأَمْرِنَا لَمَّا صَبَرُوا وَكَانُوا بِآيَاتِنَا يُوقِنُونَ

And We made from among them leaders guiding by Our command when they were patient and were certain of Our signs.

— Quran 32:24

Islamic Spain (Al-Andalus)

Muslim rule in the Iberian Peninsula from 711 to 1492 CE created a remarkable civilization known for its tolerance, learning, and architectural achievements. Cities like Cordoba and Granada became centers of knowledge.

The Spread of Islam

Islam spread through various means including trade, missionary activities, and conquest. Today, Islam is practiced by over 1.8 billion people worldwide, making it the second-largest religion globally.

"The world is green and beautiful, and Allah has appointed you as His stewards over it."

— Sahih Muslim

Chapter Eleven

Islamic Arts and Sciences

Islamic civilization has made extraordinary contributions to the arts and sciences, driven by the Islamic emphasis on seeking knowledge and understanding Allah's creation. These achievements laid foundations for modern scientific and artistic endeavors.

Scientific Achievements

Muslim scholars made groundbreaking discoveries in various fields of science, often building upon and expanding the knowledge of previous civilizations while making original contributions.

Major Fields of Islamic Scientific Contribution:

  • Mathematics - Algebra, algorithms, decimal system
  • Astronomy - Accurate star charts, astrolabes, observations
  • Medicine - Surgical techniques, pharmacology, hospitals
  • Chemistry - Laboratory equipment, distillation, chemical processes
  • Physics - Optics, mechanics, scientific method
  • Geography - World maps, navigation techniques

Notable Muslim Scientists and Scholars

Al-Khwarizmi (780-850 CE): Known as the father of algebra, his work laid the foundation for modern mathematics and computer science.

Ibn Sina (Avicenna) (980-1037 CE): His medical encyclopedia "The Canon of Medicine" was used in European universities for centuries.

Al-Biruni (973-1048 CE): Made significant contributions to astronomy, mathematics, and anthropology, accurately calculating the Earth's circumference.

Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen) (965-1040 CE): Pioneered the scientific method and made fundamental contributions to optics and physics.

وَقُل رَّبِّ زِدْنِي عِلْمًا

And say: My Lord, increase me in knowledge.

— Quran 20:114

Islamic Architecture

Islamic architecture developed distinctive features that reflect both spiritual principles and aesthetic beauty. Key elements include geometric patterns, calligraphy, and the integration of light and space.

Characteristic Features of Islamic Architecture:

  • Geometric patterns and arabesques
  • Calligraphic decorations with Quranic verses
  • Minarets and domes
  • Courtyards and gardens (reflecting paradise)
  • Mashrabiya (decorative wooden screens)
  • Use of water features for cooling and beauty

Islamic Calligraphy

Arabic calligraphy is considered the highest form of Islamic art, as it directly represents the word of Allah. Different styles evolved across regions and periods, each with its own aesthetic characteristics.

Music and Poetry

Islamic civilization produced rich traditions of poetry and music. Poets like Rumi, Hafez, and Omar Khayyam created works that continue to inspire readers worldwide. Islamic music developed sophisticated theoretical frameworks and diverse regional styles.

"Allah is beautiful and loves beauty."

— Sahih Muslim

Islam and Modern Society

Islam continues to be a vibrant and relevant faith in the modern world, with Muslims adapting Islamic principles to contemporary challenges while maintaining core beliefs and values.

Contemporary Islamic Thought

Modern Muslim scholars and thinkers work to address contemporary issues through Islamic perspectives, balancing traditional wisdom with modern realities in fields such as science, technology, economics, and social justice.

Areas of Modern Islamic Discourse:

  • Islamic finance and economics
  • Medical ethics and bioethics
  • Environmental sustainability
  • Human rights and social justice
  • Gender roles and women's rights
  • Interfaith dialogue and cooperation

Islamic Finance

Islamic finance operates according to Shariah principles, prohibiting interest (riba), excessive uncertainty (gharar), and investment in harmful industries. It emphasizes risk-sharing, asset-backing, and ethical investment.

❋

Education and Knowledge

The Islamic emphasis on seeking knowledge has led to the establishment of numerous educational institutions worldwide. Many Muslim countries have developed modern educational systems that integrate Islamic values with contemporary curricula.

يَا أَيُّهَا الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا إِذَا قِيلَ لَكُمْ تَفَسَّحُوا فِي الْمَجَالِسِ فَافْسَحُوا يَفْسَحِ اللَّهُ لَكُمْ وَإِذَا قِيلَ انشُزُوا فَانشُزُوا يَرْفَعِ اللَّهُ الَّذِينَ آمَنُوا مِنكُمْ وَالَّذِينَ أُوتُوا الْعِلْمَ دَرَجَاتٍ

Allah will raise those who have believed among you and those who were given knowledge, by degrees.

— Quran 58:11

Interfaith Relations

Islam encourages respectful dialogue and cooperation with people of other faiths. The Quran acknowledges the diversity of human beliefs and emphasizes that there should be no compulsion in religion.

Social Justice and Human Rights

Islamic principles of justice, equality, and human dignity provide a framework for addressing modern social issues. Many Muslim organizations work actively on issues such as poverty alleviation, education, healthcare, and human rights.

"All mankind is from Adam and Adam is from dust. There is no superiority of an Arab over a non-Arab, or of a non-Arab over an Arab, except by piety and good action."

— Final Sermon of Prophet Muhammad

Chapter Thirteen

Islamic Philosophy and Theology

Islamic philosophy and theology (Kalam) represent sophisticated intellectual traditions that have grappled with fundamental questions about existence, knowledge, ethics, and the nature of the divine for over a millennium.

Early Islamic Philosophy

Islamic philosophy emerged as Muslim scholars encountered Greek philosophical works and sought to reconcile reason with revelation. This led to rich debates about the relationship between faith and reason.

Major Islamic Philosophers:

  • Al-Kindi (801-873 CE) - "The Philosopher of the Arabs"
  • Al-Farabi (872-950 CE) - Known for political philosophy
  • Ibn Sina (980-1037 CE) - Metaphysician and scientist
  • Ibn Rushd (1126-1198 CE) - Defender of Aristotelian philosophy
  • Al-Ghazali (1058-1111 CE) - Critic of philosophers, mystic theologian

Schools of Islamic Theology

Several theological schools developed within Islam, each offering different approaches to understanding faith, reason, and divine attributes:

Ash'arism: Emphasized divine sovereignty and predestination while maintaining human responsibility.

Maturidism: Balanced divine sovereignty with human free will, emphasizing the role of reason in theology.

Mu'tazilism: Stressed divine justice and human free will, giving significant role to reason in understanding faith.

وَمَا خَلَقْتُ الْجِنَّ وَالْإِنسَ إِلَّا لِيَعْبُدُونِ

And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me.

— Quran 51:56

Islamic Mysticism (Sufism)

Sufism represents the mystical dimension of Islam, emphasizing the inner spiritual journey toward Allah. Sufis seek to purify the heart and achieve direct spiritual experience of the divine.

Stages of Sufi Spiritual Development:

  • Tawbah (Repentance) - Turning away from sin
  • Wara (Scrupulousness) - Avoiding doubtful matters
  • Zuhd (Asceticism) - Detachment from worldly desires
  • Sabr (Patience) - Perseverance in trials
  • Tawakkul (Trust) - Complete reliance on Allah
  • Rida (Contentment) - Satisfaction with Allah's decree

The Question of Divine Attributes

Islamic theologians extensively debated the nature of Allah's attributes, such as knowledge, power, and mercy. The central question was whether these attributes are identical to Allah's essence or distinct from it.

"Allah has ninety-nine names, one hundred minus one, and whoever encompasses them will enter Paradise."

— Sahih al-Bukhari

Chapter Fourteen

The Islamic Calendar and Festivals

The Islamic calendar, also known as the Hijri calendar, is a lunar calendar consisting of 12 months and approximately 354-355 days. It begins with the Hijra (migration) of Prophet Muhammad from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE.

The Islamic Months

The Twelve Islamic Months:

  • Muharram - The sacred month, beginning of the Islamic year
  • Safar - The empty or void month
  • Rabi' al-Awwal - The first spring month
  • Rabi' al-Thani - The second spring month
  • Jumada al-Awwal - The first dry month
  • Jumada al-Thani - The second dry month
  • Rajab - The respected month
  • Sha'ban - The month of separation
  • Ramadan - The month of fasting
  • Shawwal - The month of hunting
  • Dhul Qi'dah - The month of rest
  • Dhul Hijjah - The month of pilgrimage

Major Islamic Festivals

Eid al-Fitr (Festival of Breaking the Fast)

Celebrated on the first day of Shawwal, marking the end of Ramadan. Muslims gather for special prayers, give charity (Zakat al-Fitr), exchange gifts, and enjoy festive meals with family and friends.

Eid al-Adha (Festival of Sacrifice)

Observed on the 10th day of Dhul Hijjah, commemorating Prophet Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son in obedience to Allah. Muslims who can afford it sacrifice an animal and distribute the meat to family, friends, and the poor.

فَصَلِّ لِرَبِّكَ وَانْحَرْ

So pray to your Lord and sacrifice [to Him alone].

— Quran 108:2

Sacred Months

Four months in the Islamic calendar are considered sacred: Muharram, Rajab, Dhul Qi'dah, and Dhul Hijjah. During these months, fighting was traditionally prohibited, and spiritual activities were emphasized.

Other Important Observances

Significant Islamic Dates:

  • Ashura (10th Muharram) - Day of fasting and remembrance
  • Mawlid al-Nabi - Birthday of Prophet Muhammad
  • Laylat al-Qadr - The Night of Power during Ramadan
  • Laylat al-Mi'raj - The Night Journey of the Prophet
  • Day of Arafat (9th Dhul Hijjah) - Day of Hajj pilgrimage

The Significance of Lunar Calendar

The use of a lunar calendar means that Islamic festivals rotate through different seasons over the years, ensuring that no particular season is permanently associated with religious observances.
